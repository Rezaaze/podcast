--- PART 1 ---

From the courtroom myth of a seductress who was never real, the story turns to a broadcaster whose hatred needed no invention at all — a drifter from Belgium who found, in the hills of Rwanda, a microphone powerful enough to end lives. His name was Georges Ruggiu, and before he became the only white man ever convicted of genocide by an international tribunal, he was simply a failure, a young man from Verviers who had tried and dropped out of universities in Liège and London, who had drifted through leftist student politics without conviction, who had applied for jobs he never got and pitched radio segments no station wanted. He was, by every account, unremarkable — a voice with nowhere to land. Radio does not care about a man's résumé. It cares only about the hum of static waiting to be filled, and Ruggiu, more than anything, wanted to fill it. In 1993 he arrived in Kigali, ostensibly as a journalist, though the line between journalist and propagandist would soon dissolve so completely that even he seemed to forget it had ever existed. Rwanda, at that moment, was a nation vibrating with tension — a fragile peace accord, a Hutu-led government fearful of losing power, and an ethnic narrative decades in the making, one that had already claimed lives in smaller massacres throughout the early nineties. Into this charged air came a new radio station, one unlike the stiff, state-run broadcasts Rwandans were used to. Radio Télévision Libre des Mille Collines played pop music between political commentary. It let listeners call in. It sounded, deliberately, like a party — loud, familiar, alive. And somewhere in its offices, a Belgian outsider found the thing he had never possessed at home: an audience that would listen to him, that needed him, that made him feel, for the first time, essential. The irony would define the rest of his life. A man who had failed to find his voice in the peaceful boredom of Western Europe found it instead in the anxious, blood-warmed air of a country not his own — a foreigner who did not simply report on a nation's fracture but who would help widen it, syllable by syllable, broadcast by broadcast. Radio in Rwanda in the early 1990s was not background noise. In a country where roughly half the population could not read, where villages sat far from newspapers and television, the radio was the primary nervous system of public knowledge, the thing that arrived by mouth of a wooden box in nearly every home, market stall, and roadside bar. Whoever controlled that frequency did not merely inform Rwanda. They spoke directly into its collective mind. And Georges Ruggiu, a man who had never controlled anything in his life, was about to discover exactly how much control a single microphone could offer him.

--- PART 2 ---

To understand how a Belgian dropout became the fluent voice of Hutu extremism, you have to understand what Radio Mille Collines actually was — not a fringe pamphlet mailed to true believers, but a slick, modern, entertaining broadcast built by people who understood media better than most Western stations of the era. Founded in 1993 by figures close to the ruling Hutu elite, including relatives of President Juvénal Habyarimana, the station was engineered to feel independent, youthful, even rebellious against the stodgy state broadcaster. It mixed Congolese rumba and Zairean pop with call-in segments, gossip, sports commentary, and news, creating a rhythm of familiarity that made its poison easier to swallow. Listeners did not tune in expecting propaganda. They tuned in because it sounded like fun, and this was precisely the trap: hatred delivered on the same station that played their favorite songs did not feel like hatred at all. It felt like belonging. Ruggiu did not create this formula, but he became one of its most effective voices, in part because he was foreign. A Belgian accent carried, to Rwandan ears already primed by decades of colonial hierarchy, an unearned authority — the voice of the old colonizer now seemingly on their side, endorsing Hutu grievance with the borrowed credibility of Europe itself. He broadcast in French, reaching the educated, urban, elite audience the extremist movement most needed to legitimize itself, while Kinyarwanda-speaking colleagues worked the wider population. He gave interviews, hosted political discussions, and slowly, deliberately, began to abandon any pretense of journalistic distance. Colleagues later described a man who seemed almost giddy with his new relevance, who spoke of Tutsis using the dehumanizing language already circulating through extremist Hutu circles, comparing them to cockroaches, to snakes, to an infestation requiring eradication rather than people requiring persuasion. This was not accidental drift. It was radicalization happening in real time, on air, with an audience as witness — a man discovering that the emptiness he had carried his whole life could be filled, instantly and completely, by hatred wearing the costume of purpose. He had failed at love, at career, at every ordinary form of belonging Western life offered him. Here, in a studio in Kigali, he was finally somebody — a somebody built entirely from an ideology that treated an entire ethnic group as vermin to be cleared from the national body. What none of his listeners could yet know, and what Ruggiu himself may not have grasped, was how quickly his broadcasts would stop being commentary and start becoming instruction — a countdown clock already ticking somewhere beneath the pop songs and the call-in banter, waiting only for the right catalyst to turn radio into a weapon.

--- PART 3 ---

By late 1993 and into early 1994, the rhythm of Radio Mille Collines had shifted. The music remained, the call-in segments remained, but the language surrounding them grew sharper, more specific, more urgent. Ruggiu's broadcasts increasingly framed the political moment not as a negotiation between parties but as an existential threat. The Arusha Accords, signed in August 1993 to end the civil war between the Hutu-led government and the Rwandan Patriotic Front, were described on air not as peace but as betrayal — a document that would hand the country to what extremist voices called "the enemy within." This framing mattered enormously. It converted a political settlement into a countdown, a deadline before which, the broadcasts implied, decisive action needed to be taken. Ruggiu spoke of self-defense, of vigilance, of the need for ordinary Hutu citizens to remain alert. He rarely used the word violence directly. He did not need to. The vocabulary of extermination had already been seeded by other voices on the station and in extremist newspapers — lists of supposed Tutsi conspirators, warnings about infiltrators, reminders of the so-called Hutu Ten Commandments that had circulated since 1990, forbidding intermarriage, cooperation, even basic trust across ethnic lines. Ruggiu's contribution was to make this ideology sound reasonable in French, to give it the texture of political analysis rather than incitement, delivered in the measured tones of a man who sounded like he had walked out of a European newsroom rather than a hate campaign. Behind the scenes, the station's ownership tied it directly to the inner circle of power, the shadowy network surrounding the president's family later identified by investigators as central to genocide planning. Ruggiu, whether he grasped the full architecture or not, had placed himself inside a machine already moving toward mass violence, a machine that needed exactly what he offered: a credible, articulate, foreign voice to launder its message for the audience that mattered most, the fence-sitters, the moderates, the Hutu who had not yet decided which side of history they would stand on. Weapons were already being distributed across Kigali under the guise of civil defense. Militia groups, the Interahamwe, trained openly, their movements sometimes announced on the very airwaves Ruggiu helped fill each day. The country did not so much drift toward catastrophe as march toward it, guided in part by a rhythm broadcast daily into homes, bars, and roadside markets, a rhythm the population had learned to trust precisely because it still sounded like entertainment. Diplomats and aid workers who monitored the station noted the change in tone but underestimated its consequence, treating it as noise rather than architecture. All that remained now was a spark, a single event violent enough to collapse the fragile distance between rhetoric and action. On the evening of April 6, 1994, that spark arrived without warning, in the sky directly above Kigali.

--- PART 4 ---

At 8:23 p.m., the presidential jet carrying Juvénal Habyarimana was struck by missile fire and fell from the sky, killing everyone aboard. Within minutes, before official government statements, before any confirmed investigation, Radio Mille Collines was already on air with a story, and Ruggiu's voice was part of the machinery that told it. The narrative arrived instantly and without hesitation: the Rwandan Patriotic Front, the Tutsi rebel movement, had assassinated the president, and every Tutsi civilian was now recast, in a single broadcast sentence, as a suspect, an accomplice, an enemy combatant hiding in plain sight among neighbors and coworkers. The truth of who fired that missile remains disputed by historians and investigators to this day, but on the radio that night, certainty was manufactured within the hour. Roadblocks appeared across Kigali before sunrise. Identity cards, which under Belgian colonial policy had labeled Rwandans by ethnicity for decades, became instruments of selection at those checkpoints. Radio Mille Collines did not merely report these events. It steered them, describing troop movements, urging listeners toward vigilance, insisting that the moment for hesitation had ended. Ruggiu was present in the studio during these critical early hours, broadcasting alongside Rwandan colleagues as the killing began in earnest across the capital and then spread outward into the provinces. What had, months earlier, been suggestive language and dehumanizing metaphor now collapsed into direct address. The radio spoke of enemies who needed to be found, of work that needed to be done, and listeners who had spent a year absorbing this vocabulary understood precisely what was being asked of them without needing further clarification. Neighbors who had lived beside each other for generations began killing neighbors within days, sometimes within hours, often with the radio playing in the background of the act itself, as though it were supervising. Ruggiu, the failed student, the rejected job applicant, the man who could not find purpose anywhere in Western Europe, now had an audience larger and more obedient than he could have imagined in his most desperate fantasies of relevance. He had wanted, more than anything, to matter. In the studio at Radio Mille Collines, during those first catastrophic days of April 1994, he mattered more than almost anyone in the country, and the price of that significance was being paid in the streets, at the roadblocks, in the churches where families had gathered believing sanctuary still existed. The station did not create the genocide alone. It could not have. But it accelerated what political planning had already set in motion, compressing hesitation into action, turning rumor into targeting data in real time. And Ruggiu, still finding his footing in this new and terrible fluency, was about to discover that naming an enemy on air was only the beginning of what the radio could demand of him.

--- PART 5 ---

The radio did not stop asking. It asked more, and it asked faster. Within days of the plane crash, Radio Mille Collines had transformed from a station that gestured toward enemies into a station that pointed at them, one by one, in real time. Ruggiu was no longer describing a political climate. He was reading coordinates. Listeners called in with tips, with sightings, with names, and those names went straight to air, unfiltered, unverified, delivered in the same conversational cadence he had once used to hype a football match or spin a pop record. A car described by make and license plate. A neighborhood, a street corner, a specific house where someone was said to be hiding. The mechanics were terrifyingly simple: say it on the radio, and within the hour, someone would act on it. Ruggiu understood this. He later admitted, in testimony that would follow him for the rest of his life, that he knew precisely what happened after certain broadcasts went out. He was not guessing at consequences. He was tracking them. The station operated less like a newsroom now and more like a targeting system, staffed by announcers who had learned that hesitation cost nothing and specificity cost everything. Ruggiu leaned into that specificity with a kind of manic fluency, code-switching between French and fragments of Kinyarwanda, presenting himself increasingly as something more than a foreign sympathizer. He wanted to be seen as one of them, a convert, a true believer rather than an opportunist passing through. He spoke of Hutu solidarity as though he had been born into it, as though Belgium had never happened, as though his entire drifting, rejected, purposeless life before Kigali had simply been erased by the volume of his new devotion. This was the obsession taking full shape: not just participation in the killing, but a hunger to be recognized as essential to it. He wanted credit. He wanted to matter more than the Rwandan broadcasters beside him, more than Kantano Habimana, more than Valérie Bemeriki, the colleagues who had grown up inside the hatred he had only recently adopted. Investigators would later document specific instances where his broadcasts named individuals — a teacher, a local official, a family sheltering neighbors — shortly before those same people were killed at roadblocks manned by young men who had been listening to exactly the same frequency. The causality was not always provable in a courtroom with mathematical precision, but the pattern was undeniable, repeated across weeks that blurred into a single continuous transmission of denunciation. Ruggiu's voice, once flat and forgettable on obscure Belgian frequencies, had become an instrument capable of ending a life within the hour of its broadcast, and he had not merely accepted that power. He had started to crave it, chasing the feeling of consequence the way a man chases the only drug that has ever worked for him.

--- PART 6 ---

Behind the studio door, something else was happening, something the microphone never picked up directly but which colleagues would later describe in fragments. Ruggiu was not sleeping in any normal rhythm anymore. He drank between broadcasts, sometimes during them, his voice occasionally thickening on air in ways longtime listeners noticed without fully understanding why. The station itself operated in a state of controlled chaos — generators humming through power outages, announcers rotating through exhaustion, gunfire sometimes audible faintly beneath the studio walls as fighting moved through nearby neighborhoods. Kigali outside the building had become a landscape of roadblocks and silence punctuated by screaming, and inside the building, Ruggiu kept talking, kept naming, kept performing a confidence that increasingly looked, to those watching closely, like a man narrating his way past his own unraveling. He had spent his entire adult life failing to be believed, failing to be hired, failing to matter to anyone who counted. Now an entire nation's worst instincts hung on his inflection, and the intoxication of that was inseparable, even then, from something closer to psychological collapse. He began repeating phrases with a compulsive rhythm, doubling down on accusations even when field reports contradicted them, as though stopping the momentum of his own voice might force him to confront what the voice had already caused. Some who worked alongside him described a man performing certainty for an audience while privately grasping for something solid to hold onto, a foreign national with no real escape route, no embassy eager to shelter him, no country back home that wanted him returned. Belgium had already severed diplomatic relations with Rwanda days after the crash, evacuating its citizens, and yet Ruggiu remained, broadcasting, embedded, seemingly unable or unwilling to extract himself from the machine he had helped accelerate. Whether this was loyalty, fear, addiction to relevance, or simple absence of alternatives, the effect was the same: he stayed at the microphone as the killing intensified around him, as the roadblocks multiplied, as the death toll climbed past numbers the human mind struggles to hold. The static between broadcasts, the dead air listeners sometimes caught during shift changes, carried its own kind of dread now, a pause before the next name, the next location, the next verdict delivered in a French accent that had once sounded merely foreign and now sounded, to those who survived, like the closest thing hell had to a narrator. Ruggiu had wanted an audience his whole life. In the spring of 1994, he finally had one large enough to matter, and it was about to demand from him more than any voice, however hungry for significance, should ever be asked to give.

--- PART 7 ---

Listen. The static is not empty anymore. It carries something now — a rhythm, almost a heartbeat, ticking beneath every broadcast that leaves the Mille Collines studio. April becomes May. The killing does not slow. It accelerates, and the radio accelerates with it, as though the two have fused into a single organism, one feeding the other, neither able to stop. Ruggiu is still there. Still at the microphone. His voice thinner now, faster, edged with something that sounds almost like panic dressed up as certainty. He reads. He names. He praises. He warns. The words come out in bursts, breathless, as though he is running out of air even as he speaks. Roadblocks stretch across Kigali like a nervous system laid bare, and at each one, young men wait with machetes and radios tuned to the same frequency, waiting for the next instruction disguised as commentary. Ruggiu gives it to them. Again. Again. He does not pause to ask what happens after the broadcast ends. He already knows. He has known for weeks. And still he speaks, faster, louder, as though volume alone might drown out the knowing. Somewhere outside the reinforced walls of the studio, gunfire cracks against the hum of generators, and inside, nobody flinches anymore. This is the new normal. This is the frequency of a country tearing itself apart in real time, broadcast live, with him narrating it. He praises the killers as heroes. He calls resistance treason. He calls survival, for those still hiding, a crime waiting to be corrected. His French accent, once a curiosity, is now a signature of dread, recognized instantly by those hunting and those hunted alike. He has become something almost mythic in the wrong direction — not a man anymore, but a function of the massacre, a voice indistinguishable from the machete's edge. And yet. Beneath the performance, something is fraying. Colleagues notice it first in small things — a hesitation before a name, a stumble over a location, a night where he does not show up to his shift at all. He disappears for hours. He reappears smelling of alcohol, eyes hollow, voice cracking in a way the microphone cannot fully hide. He is not sleeping. He is not eating on any schedule that resembles a human being's. The killing has become so total, so relentless, that even the man narrating it seems to sense, somewhere beneath the bravado, that he has crossed into territory from which no ordinary life waits on the other side. Belgium will not take him back cleanly. Rwanda will not protect him once this ends. He has nowhere left to stand except in front of this microphone, and the microphone is starting to feel less like a throne and more like a trap closing slowly around a man who built it with his own hands.

--- PART 8 ---

Then the front lines move. Quietly at first. Then not quietly at all. The Rwandan Patriotic Front pushes closer to Kigali, and the radio's confident, mocking tone begins to buckle under something it has never had to manage before — fear for itself. Ruggiu's broadcasts shift almost imperceptibly, then unmistakably. The certainty cracks. The bravado thins into something closer to desperation, a man narrating not just a genocide anymore but the collapse of the very ground beneath his own feet. He can hear it coming, the way you hear a storm before you see it — first as rumor, then as retreat, then as the sound of shelling drawing nearer with each broadcast day. The studio itself becomes unstable. Equipment moves. Staff scatter. The station that once felt untouchable, embedded deep in the machinery of a state committing mass murder, now feels exposed, temporary, already becoming history even as it still transmits. Ruggiu keeps broadcasting almost to the end, as though stopping might mean admitting what has already happened cannot be undone. But by July, the fiction collapses entirely. Kigali falls. The government that sheltered him disintegrates overnight, ministers and militia leaders scattering toward the border, and Ruggiu scatters with them, one voice among thousands fleeing westward, no longer commanding anything, no longer named on air, just another man running from a war he helped narrate into existence. Somewhere in that flight — through refugee columns, through collapsing checkpoints, through a landscape littered with the physical evidence of everything the radio had only ever described in words — something inside him seems to break in a way that cannot be repaired by silence or distance. He is no longer the voice from the radio. He is a fugitive, indistinguishable from any other desperate man moving through the chaos, except that his voice is the one hundreds of thousands still hear in memory, in nightmare, in testimony not yet given. The broadcasts stop. The frequency goes dark. But something does not stop — the accounting waiting for him, the specific names, the specific dates, the specific dead who can be traced, however imperfectly, back to specific words he chose to say into a microphone because he finally, after a lifetime of irrelevance, had found an audience willing to listen. He crosses into Zaire believing, perhaps, that the war's end might also mean his own erasure back into anonymity. It will not. Somewhere ahead of him, though he cannot yet know it, an international tribunal is already being imagined, and his name — Georges Ruggiu, the foreigner, the convert, the voice — is already being written down.

--- PART 9 ---

The years in exile do not restore him. They strip him further. Georges Ruggiu moves through Central Africa as one displaced body among a million others, the voice that once filled a nation's airwaves now reduced to silence, to hiding, to the exhausting arithmetic of survival without a microphone to hide behind. He is arrested in 1997, found and detained, and the machinery that had been quietly writing his name down for years finally closes around him. He is transferred to Arusha, to the International Criminal Tribunal for Rwanda, where for the first time in years he is made to sit still, made to listen instead of speak, made to hear his own voice played back to him from tapes recovered out of the wreckage of RTLM's broadcasts. It is a strange inversion of everything he once was — the propagandist forced into an audience, listening to himself the way hundreds of thousands once listened, except now the listening comes with consequence, with the full weight of what those words unleashed hour after hour, roadblock after roadblock, name after name. Something in that confrontation seems to reach him where nothing else had. In 2000, Ruggiu does something almost none of his former colleagues from the station will do — he pleads guilty. Not to a lesser charge dressed up in careful language, but to incitement to commit genocide and to crimes against humanity, admitting in open court what the broadcasts had always denied even as they performed it, that the words were never just words, that language on a radio wave can move a machete as surely as any hand. He testifies. He names names, again, though this time toward accountability rather than death, describing the chain of command, the meetings, the deliberate machinery behind what had so often sounded, on air, like spontaneous fury. Survivors and prosecutors alike hear, for the first time, the architecture beneath the noise — how calculated it truly was, how far it reached, how many voices besides his own had built the killing floor he broadcast from. He is sentenced to twelve years, a number that will strike many as impossibly small measured against the dead his words helped mark, and the disproportion becomes its own quiet indictment of how difficult it is to weigh a voice against a life, let alone against eight hundred thousand of them. He serves his sentence largely out of public sight, cooperating further, testifying in other trials, a man whose usefulness to history now lies entirely in what he can say about the machine he once operated. But cooperation is not redemption. A guilty plea is not resurrection for a single one of the dead. And somewhere in that quiet, procedural unraveling of guilt into legal language, the deeper question remains unanswered, waiting in the space between his confession and everything it could never undo.

--- PART 10 ---

He is released in 2009, his years served, and steps out into a world that no longer has any use for the voice that once filled it with such certainty. There is no return to Belgium waiting for him, no studio, no audience, no second act. Under a new identity, in a witness protection arrangement negotiated as part of his cooperation, he disappears — not into the anonymity he might once have imagined for himself, but into something closer to erasure, a life lived carefully out of sight, far from Rwanda, far from the hills and the roadblocks and the names he once read into a microphone as though they were nothing more than weather reports. He gives interviews rarely. When he does, something in his language has changed — the fluency remains, but the certainty is gone, replaced by something quieter, more halting, a man trying and failing to explain himself to people who were not there and cannot fully hear him even when he speaks plainly. He talks of manipulation, of ideology absorbed rather than chosen, of a young man's hunger for belonging exploited by people far more calculating than himself. It is not quite an excuse. It is not quite an explanation either. It is the sound of someone still, years later, trying to locate the exact moment the man he had been became the voice that named the dying before they died. No apology he offers can reach the households that heard his voice tell them a neighbor's death was justice. No sentence served can restore what a hundred days of broadcasting helped erase. He becomes, in the end, neither monster in the way he was once feared nor man in the way he might once have hoped to be seen — something in between, a case study, a name in academic papers on hate radio, a cautionary footnote in the history of a genocide that needed no radio to happen but found, in his voice, one more efficient way to happen faster. And so the frequency that once carried his voice across the hills goes silent, joining every other channel this war of words has finally exhausted. Close the series in near silence — the hum of a dead frequency, the last echo of a voice that once commanded millions now fading into nothing. What remains, when the war ends and the broadcasts stop, is not the propaganda itself but the question it leaves behind: how thin is the line between the voice that manipulates and the mind that is manipulated? Each of these broadcasters believed they stood outside the hysteria they created — architects, not victims, of the madness in the air. Yet each was consumed by it, in courtrooms, in exile, in the long silence of conscience that followed the static. Somewhere, on some forgotten frequency, a voice is still trying to convince someone of something. The ether does not forget. It only waits for the next voice foolish enough to believe it can be mastered.
