--- PART 1 ---

The wind off Capri still smells of salt and pine when we leave it behind, that island villa where an old emperor once believed the sea itself might swallow his enemies whole. We climb now from the cliffs of the Tyrrhenian into a colder, wetter world, where the sun rarely commits to shining and the forests grow so dense that noon can feel like dusk. It is the summer of 1392, and we are on a narrow road outside Le Mans, in the kingdom of France, walking beside a column of soldiers and noblemen escorting their king toward war. The trees here are old, older than the crown he wears, and the road beneath the horses' hooves is rutted from decades of pilgrims and merchants and armies passing through. The air is thick with the smell of horse sweat, damp leather, and the metallic tang of a summer storm that refuses to break. At the center of this column rides a young man of twenty-three, pale, sweat-slicked, his eyes darting toward shadows that keep multiplying at the edges of the road. His name is Charles, sixth of that name to rule France, and for twelve years he has worn the crown like a borrowed coat, uncertain if it will ever truly fit. He has not slept well in days. He has been feverish, they say, plagued by a mind that will not settle. And then, without warning, a leper steps from the treeline and seizes his horse's bridle, hissing a warning that the king is riding toward betrayal, that dark forces surround him even now. The column does not stop. It cannot stop, not for a beggar's ravings, and so it moves on, deeper into the trees, deeper into a silence that presses against the ears like water. It is in this silence that something breaks. A page drops a lance. The clatter rings out like the crack of a blade, sudden and metallic, and the king's whole body convulses as though struck. What follows is not a cry of pain but something older, something animal, a scream that tears up from the depths of him and does not stop. He draws his sword. He turns on his own household, on men who have served him since childhood, and strikes without recognition, without mercy, as if the faces around him have become the faces of enemies only he can see. Four men will die on that forest road before the king's own body finally exhausts itself, before strong arms wrestle the blade from his grip and lay him, trembling and silent, on the packed earth. He does not know where he is. He does not know who they are. Something has entered him in that forest, or perhaps something has simply broken open, and the France that watches him carried home that night will spend the next thirty years trying to name what took its king.

--- PART 2 ---

They carry him back toward Le Mans in a cart, wrapped in blankets though the summer air is warm, because he will not stop shivering. For days he lies in near-total stillness, refusing food, refusing speech, staring at the canopy above his bed as though it might, at any moment, fall and crush him. When he finally speaks again, weeks later, it is not the voice of the young king who rode out from Paris. Something in him has receded, curled inward, and what remains speaks in fragments, in fears that make no sense to the physicians who crowd his chamber with their leeches and their prayers. He does not know his own wife. He looks at Isabeau of Bavaria, the woman who has borne him children, and asks the servants who this stranger is, why she weeps at his bedside as though she has some claim on him. He forgets, for stretches of days, that he is king at all, and must be gently reminded of his own name. But it is what comes later that the chroniclers will find hardest to write plainly, hardest to look at directly, and it will define him more than any battle. Charles becomes convinced that his body is made of glass. He believes that beneath his skin, in place of blood and bone, there is something fragile and transparent, something that the slightest touch might shatter into a thousand pieces scattered across the floor. He refuses to be embraced. He recoils from his own children's hands. He orders iron rods sewn secretly into the lining of his robes, a hidden armature meant to hold his glass frame upright, to keep him from crumbling if he should stumble or fall. He will not let anyone approach without warning, will not let a door close too firmly, will not sit near a hearth for fear the heat might crack him from within. Picture, if you can, a throne room built for the display of power, its long windows admitting cold gray light, its silence broken only by the careful, padded footsteps of servants trained never to run, never to raise their voices, never to let a chair scrape too loudly against the stone. This is the sound of the Hôtel Saint-Pol now, this hush, this held breath, this palace reorganized entirely around the terror of a man convinced he might break. Around him, in that engineered silence, the whispering has already begun, the counting of years he may or may not survive, the quiet calculations of a queen, a brother, and a pair of uncles who understand, long before Charles does, that a glass king is a king who can be held, turned, and positioned exactly where his handlers wish him to shine.

--- PART 3 ---

Word travels to Paris before the king does, and it travels wrong. A leper's curse. A demon in the trees. A blade turned on loyal men for no reason anyone can name. The capital does not wait for facts. It invents them, fast, hungry, terrified, because a mad king is a vacuum, and vacuums pull in everyone standing near the throne. Two factions are already circling before Charles even reaches his own bed. On one side: Philip, Duke of Burgundy, and his brother Jean, Duke of Berry, uncles to the king, men who ruled France once before as regents when Charles was a boy and have never quite stopped believing the job was theirs by right. On the other side: Louis, Duke of Orléans, the king's own younger brother, sharp-featured, ambitious, restless in a way that unnerves men twice his age. Louis is twenty. He has spent his whole life one heartbeat from the crown and has never expected to actually feel it that close. Now he does. Now the throne is empty in every way but name, and he is not going to let his uncles simply fold the boy king into their pocket a second time. The council convenes within days, and it is not a council anymore, not really — it is two armed camps sitting at the same table, each smiling at the other while calculating exactly how weak Charles has become and exactly how long that weakness might last. Isabeau sits among them, the queen, twenty-two years old, foreign-born, and suddenly indispensable in a way no one planned for. She does not command troops. She does not command loyalty. But she commands proximity — she is the one allowed into his chamber, the one who watches him claw at his own arms in the dark, the one who will later be asked, again and again, to certify whether her husband is lucid enough to sign, to rule, to matter. Physicians are summoned from every corner of the kingdom, and their diagnoses spiral into the absurd — bad blood, celestial imbalance, the wrath of God for some unconfessed sin. But underneath the science, a darker word moves through the corridors in whispers too dangerous to say aloud: sorcery. Someone did this to him. Someone poisoned him, or cursed him, or paid a sorcerer to unmake his mind on that forest road, and every faction has a name ready to offer if the question is ever asked out loud. Meanwhile Charles lies in his chamber not knowing any of it — not the factions, not the whispers, not the queen weeping outside his door, not his own brother pacing the corridor with a hunger he has not yet learned to hide. All the king knows is the glass beneath his skin, and the cold, and the terrible certainty that if anyone touches him wrong, he will come apart in pieces on the floor of a palace that no longer feels like his.

--- PART 4 ---

For a while, terrifyingly, he seems to recover. The fever breaks. The shaking stops. Within weeks Charles is walking the halls of the Hôtel Saint-Pol on his own legs, speaking in full sentences, asking after his children by name, and the entire court exhales in a single collective breath, desperate to believe the nightmare in the forest was exactly that — a fever, a fluke, a passing storm now blown out to sea. He resumes his duties. He signs decrees. He even jokes, a little, with the men who once had to wrestle a sword from his hand. And then, without warning, it happens again. This is the pattern that will define the next three decades of his reign and the next three decades of French history: lucidity, arriving like a mercy, and then collapsing, without notice, into something unrecognizable. In one episode he runs howling through the palace corridors, convinced unseen assassins are chasing him through the dark, and it takes a dozen men to corner him, to pin him gently to the stone floor while he shrieks that they are trying to kill him, that they have always been trying to kill him. In another, far worse, he refuses to be washed, refuses to be touched at all, for nearly five months, until the smell in his own chamber becomes something the servants can barely stand to enter, until his hair is a filthy tangle and his skin is raw beneath layers of grime nobody dares to scrub away for fear of what touching him might trigger. Two men are eventually smuggled in disguised as fools, painted and bells jingling, laughing and tumbling across the floor until the king, half-delighted, half-confused, finally lowers his guard enough to let them near him with water and cloth. It works. It is horrifying that it works. And this is the truth the court is only now beginning to absorb, in full: there is no cure coming, no exorcism, no potion, no confession that will fix this. Charles will drift for the rest of his life between these two poles — brief windows of a king who remembers his own name, and long stretches of a man convinced his body is a vessel of glass that the world is conspiring to break. Every decree he signs from now on will carry an invisible asterisk. Every treaty. Every judgment. Was the king himself when he agreed to this? Or was it Isabeau's hand guiding his, or Orléans whispering at his elbow, or Burgundy's men holding the candle steady while he traces his signature like a child copying letters he does not understand? The throne has not fallen empty. It has fallen open — and everyone with a claim to France is already reaching inside.

--- PART 5 ---

Listen closely, because this part of the story does not shout. It happens in low rooms, behind half-closed doors, in voices pitched just above a breath. The glass delusion does not fade with time. It hardens. It becomes doctrine. Charles begins to speak of his own body the way a man might speak of a relic — something ancient, something holy, something that must never, ever be jarred. He will not sit near open flame, certain the heat might warp him. He will not let servants brush too close in a corridor, terrified a shoulder against his shoulder might shatter something no physician could ever set right again. And so, quietly, without ever naming the sickness out loud, the tailors of the royal household begin to work in secret. They reinforce his garments with iron rods sewn invisibly into the lining — not armor for battle, but scaffolding for a body the king believes is made of the thinnest, most breakable material in the world. Imagine the weight of that. Imagine dressing each morning inside a cage stitched to look like silk, believing that without it your ribs might simply crack apart like a dropped bottle. He sleeps, when he sleeps at all, in chambers stripped of anything that could strike him — no low beams, no sharp furniture, guards posted not to protect him from enemies outside the walls but from the small, ordinary edges of his own home. The irony sits heavy and silent in every corner of the Hôtel Saint-Pol: a king so afraid of breaking that he has built himself a prison out of caution. Isabeau visits him in these rooms, softly, careful not to raise her voice above a murmur, because sudden sound unsettles him more than almost anything else. She learns his rhythms the way one learns the breathing of a sleeping child — when he can bear a hand near his sleeve, when he cannot bear to be looked at directly, when a single wrong word will send him folding into the corner of the room, arms wrapped around himself as if holding his own skeleton in place. She becomes, in these years, less a wife than a kind of translator, standing between the raving and the real, deciding — quietly, privately — what the outside world is permitted to know about the man behind that door. And behind her, always, the men who need his signature are waiting. Waiting in antechambers. Waiting with wax already warming beside the royal seal. Waiting for the narrow window between one collapse and the next, when Charles might hold a pen steady enough to make a mark that will bind an entire kingdom to a decision he will not remember by nightfall.

--- PART 6 ---

The seal itself becomes something almost sacred, almost obscene — pressed into red wax again and again by a hand that sometimes trembles, sometimes moves with total, eerie calm, and sometimes has to be guided, gently, finger by finger, by whoever stands closest at the moment the ink is needed. Think of what that means. Think of an entire nation's laws resting on the good faith of whichever duke happens to be standing nearest the royal hand that week. Orléans learns to read the signs the way a sailor reads weather — the particular stillness in his brother's eyes that means a lucid hour is coming, and he learns to be there, in the room, prepared, documents already drafted, the moment that window opens. Burgundy's men learn the same trick from the opposite doorway. And in between these two watchful, waiting camps, physicians keep coming, one after another, quieter with each visit, because none of them can offer anything but soft words and softer promises. One suggests bleeding him, again. Another suggests prayer, longer prayer, more desperate prayer. A few, very carefully, in voices barely above a whisper themselves, suggest something no one wants written down — that perhaps this illness was placed on him deliberately, that somewhere in this court a hand mixed a poison, or paid a sorcerer, or simply wished hard enough and with enough malice for this exact catastrophe. No one is arrested. No one is even formally accused. But the suspicion settles into the walls of the palace like damp, unseen, impossible to scrub out, turning every glance between Orléans and Burgundy's men into something colder than mere rivalry. Meanwhile Charles himself grows almost fond of his affliction in its stranger moments — there are days he insists he is not Charles at all, that he has no name, that he is simply a shape someone else must feed and dress and carry through the hours. He forgets his wife's face. He does not recognize his own children when they are brought before him, small and frightened, unsure whether to bow or to weep. And yet, in the space of an afternoon, something shifts, some private door swings open behind his eyes, and he is Charles again — gentle, apologetic, aching with shame for whatever he cannot remember having done. It never lasts. It was never going to last. And somewhere in the space between his glass body and his crumbling mind, France itself has quietly begun to fracture too, its two great houses circling ever closer, waiting only for the seal to fall still for good.

--- PART 7 ---

Listen. Listen to the silence before it breaks.

By fourteen-oh-seven, the court has stopped pretending. Two houses. Two banners. One broken king between them, folded into his glass and his forgetting, unable to rule, unable even to remember that ruling was ever his to do. Louis of Orléans — the king's brother, restless, brilliant, hungry — has spent these years gathering power the way a man gathers kindling, piece by piece, quietly, until the pile is high enough to burn a kingdom down. He holds the regency in all but name. He holds Isabeau's confidence, some whisper more than her confidence. He holds taxes, troops, the ear of a mad king in his rare and precious lucid hours. And across the widening gulf of the court, Duke John of Burgundy watches him do it. Watches, and says nothing. Watches, and smiles at feasts, clasps hands, embraces his cousin before witnesses with the warmth of true kinship. It means nothing. It has never meant anything. Behind that smile something else is already moving — slow, patient, merciless.

November. Paris at dusk. Cold settling into the stones like a held breath.

Louis of Orléans is summoned — or believes he is summoned — to the queen's residence, a message carried to him that seems ordinary, seems safe. He mounts his mule in the near-dark, a handful of servants around him, torchlight throwing long shadows down the Rue Vieille-du-Temple. Listen to the quiet of that street. Listen to how empty it suddenly feels. And then — men. Fifteen, eighteen, more, spilling from a shuttered house, blades already drawn, faces already decided. There is no negotiation. There is no warning shouted into the dark. Only steel, and the terrible speed of it, and a prince of France pulled from his mount and cut down in the mud before he can even cry out his own name. His hand — severed at the wrist — lies apart from his body, still wearing its ring. Somewhere close by, a boy watches from a window and will remember this for the rest of his life. The killers do not linger. They vanish into side streets prepared in advance, torches doused, a city that will search for them and find nothing, because nothing is what they are meant to find.

By morning, Paris knows only that Orléans is dead. Butchered in the street like an animal, left in his own blood beneath a sky that gave no warning.

And somewhere behind the walls of the Hôtel Saint-Pol, in a room stripped of edges and sharp things, a king who cannot be told the truth waits to be told nothing at all — while the men around him already know, already understand, that whoever ordered this killing has just made a decision from which there will be no walking back.

--- PART 8 ---

Three days. That is how long the silence lasts.

Three days of Paris holding its breath, of rumor crawling through markets and monasteries, of a widow — Valentina Visconti — kneeling before an uncle king who barely comprehends her grief, begging for justice against a killer no one yet dares to name aloud. And then, in front of the royal council, in front of men who must have felt the floor tilt beneath them, Duke John of Burgundy simply says it. Confesses it. Not with shame — with something closer to pride, chin lifted, voice steady, as if daring the room to flinch first. He had it done, he tells them. He had his own cousin murdered in the street. And he is not finished — because within weeks he produces a theologian, one Jean Petit, who stands before the court and argues, at astonishing, chilling length, that this killing was not murder at all. That it was justice. That a tyrant had been removed, sanctioned, even holy. Listen to how easily the language turns. Listen to how quickly blood becomes doctrine when the man holding the knife also holds the pulpit.

No arrest follows. No trial, no rope, no reckoning. Burgundy walks free from that chamber, and something in the marrow of France cracks open for good.

Because now there are sides. Real sides, with real names. The Armagnacs — rallying behind Orléans's young heir and his fierce father-in-law, the Count of Armagnac — gather in fury and mourning, swearing vengeance in blood-soaked oaths. The Burgundians hold Paris, hold the mob's uneasy favor, hold a duke who has just proven he will kill a prince of the blood and call it righteousness. Between them, always, always, the king — carried from room to room, dressed and undressed, told nothing, understanding less, his signature still the only thing either side truly needs. He does not choose a faction. He cannot. Some days he does not know a war has begun in his own name. And so the armies begin to move without him, the two houses begin to burn each other's lands, hang each other's men, while the throne that should command them all sits empty in every way that matters — a chair with a body in it and no one, no one at all, steering the kingdom from behind those glass, trembling eyes.

Listen, now, to how quiet the palace grows. Listen to how the whispering has stopped being whispering at all, and become something closer to war drums — because the seal has fallen still, and France, unwatched, unruled, has begun, at last, to tear itself apart.

--- PART 9 ---

Listen, now, to the years that follow — because they do not resolve, they only lengthen, the way grief lengthens a night no one wants to end.

For seven years the civil war grinds through the fields of France, town by town, orchard by orchard, each side burning what the other side loves. And into that wound, in the autumn of fourteen-fifteen, walks a foreign king with a small, exhausted army and a claim four generations old. Near a muddy plateau named Agincourt, the flower of French chivalry — heavy in armor, deep in mud, already weakened by its own quarrels — is cut down in an afternoon by English arrows and English discipline. Thousands fall. Great names of Orléans and Bourbon and Alençon are killed or dragged away in chains. And somewhere behind locked doors in Paris, a king who might once have led that army, who might once have died among his own knights rather than in his own bed, does not even know the battle has been fought.

Listen to how small he has become by now. The glass has not left him — some days he still refuses touch, refuses linen, believes still that a hand laid wrong against his ribs will shatter him into pieces on the floor. His hollow, his silences, they empty the throne long after the guns fall silent.

By fourteen-twenty, Isabeau — his own queen, mother to the boy who should inherit everything — signs her name beneath a treaty at Troyes that does what thirty years of madness alone could never do: it disinherits her own son. The dauphin, young Charles, is declared unfit, some whisper illegitimate, cut out of the succession by his own mother's hand, while the crown of France is promised instead to Henry the Fifth of England, husband now to the king's own daughter Catherine.

Listen to what that means. Listen to how completely the body has been carved apart from within — a king still breathing, still crowned, made to watch, or perhaps mercifully unable to understand, as his own wife signs his kingdom into a stranger's house.

He is wheeled out for the ceremony. Dressed, propped, presented like a relic already half a ghost. He says the words asked of him. He does not resist. He may not even know what he has done. Around him, the men who orchestrated it — Burgundians triumphant, Armagnacs already regrouping in fury around a disinherited boy — watch a hollowed king seal his own family's ruin with a hand too tired to shake.

And somewhere in a small, curtained room, past midnight, an old man who once believed his bones were glass lies very still, listening to his own breath, wondering, perhaps, in whatever fragment of himself still remains, if this — finally — is the moment he will break.

--- PART 10 ---

He does not have long left to wait.

In the late summer of fourteen-twenty-two, in a small hunting lodge outside Paris, Henry the Fifth of England — the conqueror of Agincourt, the man named heir to the throne of France in his own son's place — dies suddenly of dysentery, not yet thirty-six years old, leaving an infant son to inherit two crowns he can neither hold nor understand. And within two months, in the palace of the Hôtel Saint-Pol, in a room grown used by now to silence, King Charles the Sixth of France follows him.

He dies quietly. Almost unnoticed, in the way a candle gutters rather than the way a fire consumes. Thirty years, almost to the month, since a hot road outside Le Mans and a stranger's hand on his bridle and a voice that told him not to go further. Thirty years since the glass first entered his mind and never fully left it. He is fifty-three years old, and by the reckoning of his own court, he has scarcely ruled a single one of those years as himself.

Listen to the strange, bitter mercy of what follows. The people of Paris — starved, exhausted, governed for three decades by regents and dukes and a queen who bartered away her son — come out into the streets to mourn him. Not the English claimant. Not the feuding houses. Him. The broken king. Chroniclers write that the crowds wept harder for Charles than they ever did for kings who reigned in full command of their senses, as if some old, wordless part of the city understood that he, too, had been a prisoner in that palace — as much a hostage of the crown as any subject starved by its wars.

His son, the disinherited dauphin, will spend the next years fighting to reclaim a kingdom his own mother signed away — a fight a peasant girl named Joan will one day carry to the gates of Orléans, though that story belongs to another throne entirely.

For Charles the Sixth, there is only this: thirty years inside a body he believed was glass, a mind that fractured on a forest road and never fully reassembled, a crown that became, in the truest sense, a cage with no door. The Sovereign of the Silent Halls, the histories will one day call him — a king who reigned by paper and seal while somewhere behind locked doors, a frightened man waited only to be told he was safe.

He was never told. Not once, not really. And so the halls fall silent behind him.

But history does not rest. From the shattered king of France, our story now turns east — past crumbling empires and gilded thrones — to a palace in Constantinople, where a prince has spent his entire life raised in the shadow of the executioner's silk cord, waiting, watching, praying he survives long enough to inherit an empire he was never meant to rule.
