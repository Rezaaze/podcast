--- SCENE 1 ---
1. It is the next morning, roughly twelve hours after episode three's close, and Julian is first into the house before anyone else is up.
2. An overnight notice on his desk demands repayment of a mortgage debt through a company name he doesn't recognize, the amount far larger than any routine payment he's handled before.
3. The notice gives him a short window to respond, leaving no time to raise it through normal banking channels without questions from Marguerite or the board.
4. He doesn't know the company is one of Marcus Tang's shells, but its timing against Marcus's approaches to Marguerite unsettles him enough that he suspects a connection he can't yet prove.
5. He decides to handle it privately before telling anyone the real amount, telling himself he needs a plan first, not panic from his mother or his wife.
6. The audience, already knowing Marcus ordered this exact call-in, watches Julian meet its consequence without recognizing its true source.

--- SCENE 2 ---
1. Later that morning, Julian finds Marguerite still turning over Marcus's pointed remark from the night before, though she says nothing of it.
2. He tells her only that a debt payment has come due earlier than planned, calling it a cash flow matter and nothing more.
3. Marguerite, relieved to hear something ordinary after her own private fear, accepts the explanation without pressing for details.
4. Julian deliberately withholds the amount, the unfamiliar company's name, and any mention of Marcus, judging it not yet certain enough to alarm her.
5. Marguerite, in turn, says nothing about Marcus's hint about the family's "complicated" history, each protecting the other from a danger that may be the same one.
6. The audience watches the two come nearer than ever to comparing notes and watches them talk past each other instead.

--- SCENE 3 ---
1. Anna brings a damaged frame to Simon's workshop as pretext, using the visit to ask, carefully, whether he's ever heard the name Lin Wei-Chen in connection with the house's early history.
2. Simon recognizes the name as his own father's and keeps his hands steady on the frame, answering in a way that confirms nothing and denies nothing outright.
3. He redirects the conversation to the technical work of the repair itself, giving her real expertise while giving away no part of himself.
4. Anna notices the half-second hesitation before he answered but reads it as unfamiliarity with old paperwork rather than concealment.
5. She thanks him and leaves the name unresolved in her notebook, no closer to knowing how close she came.
6. The audience watches Simon survive the exact question he's spent fifty years preparing to deflect, more shaken by it than his manner shows.

--- SCENE 4 ---
1. That afternoon, Julian meets the private lawyer at the waterfront, the same contact he's used for the quiet ledger review with Anna.
2. He asks the lawyer to arrange a personal loan against the family home, specifying no bank paperwork that could reach Marguerite or Nora through ordinary channels.
3. The lawyer confirms it can be done quickly but warns the terms will be worse than a standard loan given the urgency.
4. Julian accepts anyway, calculating that speed and privacy matter more right now than cost.
5. He tells himself this is temporary, a way to buy time until the debt and the ledger question are both resolved on his own terms.
6. He still suspects, without proof, that the shell company behind the call-in traces back to Marcus Tang, though neither he nor the lawyer can confirm it.

--- SCENE 5 ---
1. Before the house opens, Nora finds Julian alone in the empty salesroom and asks him directly what he isn't telling her.
2. She means the receipt and the loan letter she's carried since the day before, though she frames the question broadly rather than naming either.
3. Julian, hearing the question as being about the ledgers, answers about that instead, admitting he's been quietly digging deeper into the 1951 entries with outside professional help.
4. Nora, braced for a confession about another woman, is thrown by his answer and for a moment doubts her own read of the receipt and the loan.
5. Julian, relieved she seems to accept the partial truth, doesn't realize he's answered a different question than the one she actually asked.
6. Nora leaves with her affair suspicion and her ledger worry now tangled together rather than separated, more unsettled than before.

--- SCENE 6 ---
1. In the archive, working around restrictions that still leave her partial access, Anna lays the 1938 partnership valuation figures beside the 1951 appraisal sums for the same lots.
2. She calculates the difference precisely for the first time and finds the later figures inflated by roughly sixty percent, too consistent a margin to be error.
3. She cross-references the affected lots against the partnership paperwork and confirms every altered entry once required two signatures, not the one now on record.
4. She privately concludes, for herself alone, that the ledgers were deliberately altered rather than carelessly transcribed.
5. She still doesn't connect the name Lin Wei-Chen in the partnership paperwork to the same name already sitting in her own family notebook.
6. The audience watches Anna reach the professional truth of the forgery while standing exactly one step from the personal truth of her own family.

--- SCENE 7 ---
1. At Meridian Auctions, Marcus meets with a senior associate to review the timeline for pressuring Marguerite toward a sale.
2. The associate questions why he's moving so aggressively on a house that isn't clearly undervalued enough to justify the risk.
3. Marcus tells her just enough about his grandfather Wing-Ho Tang's lost 1952 bid to explain that this isn't purely a business calculation for him.
4. He stops short of revealing the full extent of what he suspects about the forged ledger, keeping even his own senior staff at partial knowledge.
5. The associate, now understanding the personal stake, agrees to keep pressing the timeline without further question.
6. The audience sees Marcus reveal more of his motive than he has to anyone else so far, while still guarding the deepest part of what he knows.

--- SCENE 8 ---
1. That afternoon, Nora sits with Marguerite over tea she doesn't drink and admits her suspicion about Julian and Anna Lin has grown rather than eased.
2. She recounts the strange salesroom exchange, telling Marguerite that Julian answered something about ledgers and outside help instead of denying an affair outright.
3. Marguerite, already privately holding both Marcus's hint and her own unease about the ledgers, feels the two threads pull closer together but still doesn't say so aloud.
4. She reassures Nora gently without revealing the archive restrictions or Marcus's insinuation, keeping her earlier promise of silence intact.
5. Nora leaves feeling less alone but no closer to an answer, unaware how much more Marguerite is quietly carrying beside her.
6. The audience watches Marguerite come within one honest sentence of connecting Nora's fear to the ledger truth, and watches her hold back anyway.

--- SCENE 9 ---
1. After closing, alone in his workshop, Simon sits with his father's letters spread out as he has many nights before.
2. He finds a photograph of Anna among his things that he realizes he must have taken without fully deciding to, caught in an unguarded moment during her work.
3. He sets it beside the old photograph of his father from 1938, studying the same set of the jaw, the same steadiness in the eyes across three generations.
4. He replays her question about Lin Wei-Chen from earlier that day and how close his careful answer came to breaking.
5. He doesn't move for a long time, caught between fifty years of practiced silence and a growing sense that he no longer has as much time left to keep it as he assumed.
6. The audience feels Simon standing at the furthest point yet from his old resolve, nearer to telling her than he has ever let himself be.

--- SCENE 10 ---
1. Back at the house that evening, Julian sits at his desk finalizing the loan papers the private lawyer arranged.
2. He hears Nora's footsteps in the hallway and sweeps the papers into a drawer seconds before she enters, looking for an unrelated household ledger.
3. Nora notices his slightly too-quick movement but says nothing, her confusion from the earlier salesroom conversation still unresolved.
4. She finds what she came for and leaves, unaware she came within seconds of finding the one document that would have dismantled her affair theory entirely.
5. Julian exhales once she's gone, telling himself he'll explain everything once the debt and the ledgers are both settled.
6. The audience recognizes this as the closest Nora has come yet to a truth that would end her suspicion outright.

--- SCENE 11 ---
1. That night, Anna walks out to the empty pier where she first arrived at the season's start, the old photograph of Lin Wei-Chen still in her coat pocket.
2. She turns over what she's confirmed in the archive — a deliberate forgery, a missing second signature — against Simon's careful hesitation earlier that day.
3. She admits to herself, for the first time, that her professional appraisal and her personal family research no longer feel entirely separate.
4. She decides the next conversation with Simon can't wait any longer and resolves to ask him directly rather than around the edges.
5. She still doesn't consciously suspect he is her own grandfather, only that he knows more than he's said.
6. The audience, holding the full truth of what that conversation could reveal, watches Anna step toward it without any sense of how close she already stands.
