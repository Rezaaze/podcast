--- SCENE 1 ---
1. It is the next morning, roughly ten hours after Julian's late-night confession to Marguerite at the close of episode five, and she is alone in the gallery before the rest of the household wakes.
2. Marguerite sits with the collection she has always called Augustin's solo triumph, unable to see it the same way now that she knows what he did to secure it.
3. She decides privately that she wants the matter resolved quietly, through some form of negotiated restitution to Simon and Anna, rather than let it become a public reckoning that could sink the house.
4. She tells herself Anna, still new to the family's history, might be persuaded to accept a private settlement in place of a public accounting, not recognizing how little standing Anna alone actually has to make that trade.
5. She resolves to approach Anna herself, directly and quietly, before saying anything to Julian about what she intends to offer.
6. The audience, knowing Anna has no authority to decide anything on Simon's behalf, watches Marguerite build her plan on a misunderstanding she doesn't yet see.

--- SCENE 2 ---
1. Later that morning at the auction house, Julian turns over the previous night from every angle - Marguerite's shaken silence, Nora's certainty as she walked away from the closed door.
2. He recognizes that Nora now believes, more firmly than ever, that his secrecy has been about an affair with Anna, and that nothing he's said so far has corrected it.
3. He decides the ledger crisis and the buyout debt are no longer excuses for waiting, and that Nora deserves the full truth today, whatever it costs him.
4. He considers looping in Marguerite first, then decides this conversation is his to have with his wife alone, without managing it through anyone else.
5. He leaves work early, choosing to go home and speak to Nora directly rather than let another night of silence pass between them.

--- SCENE 3 ---
1. At home that afternoon, Julian sits Nora down and tells her, without hedging, that there has never been anyone else.
2. He explains the meetings with Anna Lin were about reviewing ledger evidence with a private lawyer, not about her personally, and tells her why he kept it from her.
3. He tells her about the personal loan against the house, taken out to cover a debt payment called in by a company he now suspects is tied to Marcus Tang.
4. He tells her the ledger discrepancies she first found have turned out to be a real, deliberate forgery, older and larger than either of them first understood.
5. He admits he hid all of it not to protect a secret from her, but because he panicked and didn't want to frighten her before he understood the scale of it himself.
6. Nora hears the hotel receipt and the loan letter she's carried in silence finally explained in full, in one conversation instead of two separate fears.

--- SCENE 4 ---
1. Nora believes him - the explanation accounts for every piece of evidence she's collected too precisely to be anything but true.
2. The relief she feels is immediate and real, but underneath it she finds the hurt of the last several weeks hasn't simply disappeared along with the explanation.
3. She tells Julian she believes him, without yet telling him that the relief and the hurt are sitting side by side rather than one replacing the other.
4. Julian, reading her belief as the end of the crisis between them, doesn't notice she's holding something back.
5. Nora decides she needs time before she can talk about the weeks of silence itself, separate from what he was silent about.
6. The audience sees a marriage that has just cleared its worst misunderstanding and still has real repair work ahead of it.

--- SCENE 5 ---
1. In the workshop, Simon and Anna sit together in the quiet that's followed since he told her everything.
2. Anna says she isn't ready to decide anything about what the family owes them, only that she needed to hear the truth from him directly.
3. Simon tells her the choice of what happens next was always going to be hers to help make, not his to hand down or withhold the way he once withheld the truth itself.
4. They talk carefully about what kind of relationship they want now - grandfather and granddaughter first, before any question of the ledgers or the house.
5. Anna doesn't tell Simon how angry she's grown on his behalf, not wanting him to feel responsible for carrying her feelings on top of his own fifty years of silence.
6. The audience watches two people choosing each other in the middle of a crisis neither of them started.

--- SCENE 6 ---
1. Marguerite comes to the workshop that afternoon seeking out Anna specifically, not as the insurer's appraiser but as one family speaking to the wronged one.
2. She tells Anna she knows the truth Julian uncovered and that she wants to make it right, quietly, before the house's history has to be reckoned with in public.
3. She proposes something private - compensation, recognition within the family's own records, some measure of restoring what was taken, kept out of any formal or public accounting.
4. She frames it as respect for Simon's fifty years of silence, though underneath it she is protecting the house's name and the museum loan as much as she is protecting Simon and Anna.
5. Anna listens without answering, aware that Marguerite is asking her to decide something on behalf of people who aren't in the room.
6. The audience recognizes Marguerite's offer as sincere and also as exactly the private containment she decided on alone that morning, never having said so to Julian.

--- SCENE 7 ---
1. Anna tells Marguerite she won't accept or refuse anything on the spot, and that no arrangement can be hers alone to make.
2. She says plainly that this is Simon's history and Simon's stake, not something she can trade away or accept on his behalf without asking him first.
3. She admits only to herself that she still doesn't know what she actually wants from this - vindication, her grandfather's comfort, or something she hasn't named yet.
4. Marguerite, hearing the refusal, realizes for the first time that her plan for a quiet resolution depends on an authority Anna doesn't have and isn't willing to claim.
5. Anna tells Marguerite she'll speak with Simon before anything is decided, leaving the offer open but unanswered.
6. The audience watches Marguerite's private assumption about Anna's willingness to settle collapse in real time, without either woman naming what happens if Simon says no.

--- SCENE 8 ---
1. At Meridian Auctions, Marcus receives word from a contact close to the Vantry family's insurer, someone with visibility into Anna's re-appraisal findings.
2. The contact confirms, in terms Marcus can trust, that a forgery scandal is real and already unfolding inside the Vantry house.
3. Marcus doesn't learn the full extent of what Simon and Anna can prove, only enough to know his suspicion was correct and the timeline has changed.
4. He keeps the source and the full extent of what he now knows to himself, unwilling to let even his own staff see how thin his information still is.
5. He recognizes this confirmation as the leverage he's been building toward for two years, arriving sooner than he expected.

--- SCENE 9 ---
1. Marcus weighs whether to keep pressuring Marguerite privately or force the scandal into the open himself.
2. He decides a scandal that breaks on the Vantrys' own terms, quietly and slowly, does him no good - it needs to surface when it damages the family's value most and makes his offer look like a rescue.
3. He resolves to control the timing of the exposure himself, rather than wait for Marguerite, Julian, or the museum-loan deadline to force it out first.
4. He keeps this intention hidden even from his own inner circle, letting them believe he is still simply waiting on Marguerite to break.
5. The audience understands Marcus's next move will not be another private approach to Marguerite, even though Julian, unaware of this, still expects exactly that.

--- SCENE 10 ---
1. Back at the auction house that evening, Julian and Nora move through the gallery together for the first time since his confession, careful with each other in a way that's new.
2. Small gestures replace the words neither is ready to say yet - Julian checking in without prompting, Nora staying rather than retreating to another room.
3. Neither raises the unresolved question of Marcus Tang's shell companies or how much of the debt still presses on the house.
4. Nora tells Julian she believes him completely, and separately, that she needs time before things feel normal between them again, and he doesn't push past that line.
5. The audience sees a couple rebuilding something real, one careful exchange at a time, while the larger crisis around them keeps moving without waiting for them to catch up.

--- SCENE 11 ---
1. Narration turns to the charity gala now three days away, long planned as the house's public reassurance to Esmond Harbor's lenders and patrons ahead of the museum loan.
2. The family that once prepared for it as routine now faces it with a forgery confirmed, a debt half-hidden, a granddaughter's claim unresolved, and a rival who may already be moving to force the truth into the open before they're ready.
3. Marguerite must decide whether to attend as the house's proud, untroubled face or prepare, quietly, for the possibility that Anna and Simon's answer - or Marcus's timing - arrives before the guests do.
4. Julian and Nora must present themselves as a settled couple in public while still finding their footing in private.
5. Simon and Anna have three days to decide, together, what if anything they want said or shown at an event built on the very story Simon has spent fifty years not telling.
6. The audience is left counting down alongside every character, aware that the gala meant to buy the family time may instead be the moment all four converging threads reach the same room at once.
