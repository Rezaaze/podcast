--- SCENE 1 ---
1. It is the first week of the museum-loan re-appraisal season, early autumn, the season's opening beat with no prior episode to reference.
2. Marguerite Vantry walks the gallery floor with the forty-seven lots of the Founding Collection freshly uncrated and laid out under glass for cataloguing.
3. Marguerite speaks of Augustin Vantry's founding of the collection as a solo triumph of taste and nerve, believing this completely.
4. Nora Vantry, present to begin the archival cross-check the insurer requires, listens without correcting her, since Nora does not yet doubt the story either.
5. Julian Vantry stops by only briefly, calling the re-appraisal routine paperwork before the loan can proceed, distracted by something he doesn't name.
6. The audience is shown the scale and pride of the collection so that later cracks in its origin story will register as a loss.

--- SCENE 2 ---
1. Alone that evening, Nora begins comparing 1951 appraisal ledger signatures against other known Augustin Vantry handwriting samples in the archive.
2. She notices one entry's signature sits subtly wrong — the loops and pressure don't match Augustin's hand elsewhere.
3. She checks the recorded appraisal sum against comparable lots from the same period and finds it looks inflated, though she can't yet say by how much.
4. Nora decides to say nothing to anyone yet, wanting certainty before she raises an alarm that could prove nothing.
5. She tells herself it's likely a bookkeeping anomaly or a later re-transcription, not evidence of anything deliberate.
6. The audience senses, from how carefully Nora rechecks her own work, that she suspects more than she's letting herself believe.

--- SCENE 3 ---
1. Anna Lin arrives by ferry at Esmond Harbor's pier, travel case in hand, here to begin her insurer-assigned reassessment of the Founding Collection.
2. She pauses on the pier to look at an old photograph she carries of a man — Lin Wei-Chen — standing beside crated porcelain in 1938.
3. She doesn't yet know the man in the photograph is connected to the very house she's about to enter for work.
4. She tells herself any family connection to auction houses is probably coincidental, generic old-world history, nothing tied to this specific place.
5. The audience, already holding the truth of her lineage, watches her walk toward the house unaware of what waits for her there.

--- SCENE 4 ---
1. Julian and Marguerite greet Anna Lin at the house's entrance, treating her as a courteous but faintly unwelcome formality tied to the insurer's demands.
2. Marguerite recites the same proud founding-story of Augustin Vantry's solo genius, this time to a stranger who will actually scrutinize it.
3. Julian is polite but visibly elsewhere, his mind on something he hasn't named even to himself yet.
4. Anna notices Julian's distraction and Marguerite's faint unease but reads both as ordinary family stress, not knowing about Marcus Tang's approach or Nora's find.
5. Marguerite privately hopes the re-appraisal will be the routine formality it's always been before, unaware how untrue that hope may prove.

--- SCENE 5 ---
1. Anna's first stop on her appraisal tour is the workshop of the old restorer, Simon Lin, tucked at the back of the house.
2. Simon looks up at her arrival and recognizes her name and her resemblance to his own mother almost instantly.
3. He says nothing about it, greeting her only as a professional colleague come to assess the collection.
4. Anna finds him warm and easy to talk to, with no idea he is her own grandfather.
5. Simon feels a strange pull toward her that he conceals from her and from everyone else in the house.
6. The audience, knowing what Simon knows, feels the gap between his composed manner and what he's holding back.

--- SCENE 6 ---
1. Across the harbor, Marcus Tang reviews his partial copy of the 1951 ledger's inconsistencies, weighing when to move.
2. He confirms with his staff that the last of the minority-shareholding cousins' stakes and the mortgage debt are now fully in his control.
3. He decides the museum-loan re-appraisal gives him a narrowing window to secure a quiet sale before any public exposure forces the Vantrys' hand.
4. He does not yet know how close Nora Vantry's independent discovery already is to the same discrepancies he's been collecting.
5. His approach to Marguerite is framed internally as leverage, not yet as full disclosure of what he suspects about the forgery.

--- SCENE 7 ---
1. Marcus Tang meets Marguerite Vantry privately and proposes, courteously, an investment or sale arrangement for the house.
2. He presents his interest as ordinary business opportunism, giving no hint of his family's grievance or his knowledge of the ledger discrepancies.
3. Marguerite accepts the meeting graciously but is more unsettled by it than she shows, even to herself.
4. She tells herself afterward that his interest is purely ordinary ambition, nothing personal or historical, though the meeting lingers uncomfortably.
5. She decides not to mention the full weight of her unease to her own family, only a passing, minimized version of it.

--- SCENE 8 ---
1. The family gathers for dinner, performing an ease none of them fully feel.
2. Marguerite mentions Marcus Tang's overture only in passing, as a minor courtesy call not worth much discussion.
3. Julian, hearing it, dismisses it internally as easily ignored, unaware of the scale of what Marcus actually controls.
4. Nora watches Julian across the table, noting his distance and distraction without voicing her growing worry.
5. Nora tells herself it's simply work stress from the re-appraisal season, nothing more, though the explanation feels thinner each night.
6. The audience sees every family member holding back a different piece of the truth from the others at the same table.

--- SCENE 9 ---
1. Julian comes home late again, preoccupied, offering only a vague excuse about paperwork at the house.
2. Nora notices the same evasiveness as the nights before and says nothing, unwilling to voice her suspicion yet.
3. While hanging up his coat, a hotel receipt slips into view — one from an evening Julian spent with Anna Lin and a private lawyer reviewing ledger evidence, though Nora doesn't know that context.
4. Nora quietly pockets the receipt rather than confronting him immediately, her worry sharpening into something closer to suspicion.
5. Julian, unaware the receipt has been seen, continues to believe there's nothing yet worth explaining to Nora since he doesn't fully understand what's bothering him himself.
6. The audience, knowing the receipt's true innocent context, feels the gap widening between what Nora will conclude and what actually happened.

--- SCENE 10 ---
1. Late that night, Nora returns alone to the archive to look again at the 1951 ledger entry that troubled her.
2. She lays the hotel receipt beside the ledger pages, the two separate anxieties now sitting on the same table in her mind.
3. She begins to suspect the secrecy, the woman, and the financial strain she's sensed in Julian might connect to something bigger than an affair, though she can't yet say what.
4. She still holds back from telling anyone — not Marguerite, not Julian — wanting more certainty before she speaks.
5. The audience sees Nora standing at the exact intersection of two different truths she cannot yet tell apart.

--- SCENE 11 ---
1. The episode closes on quiet, unresolved threads: a signature that doesn't match, a receipt that means the wrong thing, an offer that isn't what it claims to be, a granddaughter who doesn't know her own grandfather.
2. Simon Lin, alone in his workshop, looks at the correspondence and ledger pages he has kept hidden for decades and does not touch them.
3. Marguerite, alone, allows herself a moment of the unease she hid from her family at dinner.
4. Marcus Tang notes with satisfaction that his first approach landed exactly as intended, still unaware of how close Nora's re-appraisal already runs to his own evidence.
5. The audience is left holding every piece the characters don't yet have — the true 1951 forgery, the true reason for Julian's distraction, and the true reason Anna Lin's photograph matters — as the house settles for the night still believing its own story.
