--- SCENE 1 ---
1. Two evenings after the dinner where Marcus Tang's overture was mentioned in passing, Nora finally brings her ledger discovery to Julian in private, done waiting for certainty.
2. She lays the mismatched signature and the inflated appraisal sum side by side, walking him through exactly what she found and how she found it.
3. Julian goes quiet in a way that surprises her — she expected him to wave it off, not to look as rattled as he does.
4. Nora watches his reaction and wonders, for the first time, whether his weeks of distraction and this discovery might be connected.
5. Julian asks her not to mention it to Marguerite yet, framing it as caution rather than concealment, and Nora agrees, relieved to share the weight of it with someone.
6. Neither mentions the hotel receipt Nora is still carrying, each holding a separate silence across the same table.

--- SCENE 2 ---
1. The next day, Julian goes to the archive alone, telling Marguerite he's only checking routine insurance paperwork.
2. He pulls the same 1951 ledger Nora flagged and works through it page by page against other Vantry records from the same era.
3. He finds several more entries with the same wrong-feeling signature, the pattern reaching further than what Nora originally showed him.
4. He tells himself it could still be a later re-transcription, some clerk's careless copying, rather than let himself think the word forgery.
5. The scale of what he's finding unsettles him more than he shows, and he begins to grasp this could touch the whole collection's legitimacy.
6. He decides he needs someone with real appraisal expertise to confirm what he's seeing before he does anything else.

--- SCENE 3 ---
1. Julian returns from the archive and finds Marguerite in the gallery, admiring the uncrated collection with the same certainty she's always had.
2. He almost tells her what he and Nora have found, listening to her speak of Augustin's genius as unquestioned fact.
3. He stops himself, unwilling to be the one who breaks that certainty before he even understands what he's looking at.
4. He decides to keep investigating quietly first, telling himself he's protecting her rather than hiding from her.
5. He also decides to reach Anna Lin privately rather than through the house's formal appraisal channel, wanting no record of the question.
6. Marguerite mentions offhand that Marcus Tang has asked for a second meeting, and Julian barely registers it, his mind already elsewhere.

--- SCENE 4 ---
1. Julian meets Anna Lin privately at the waterfront that evening, away from the house and from anyone who might mention it to Nora or Marguerite.
2. He shows her copies of the ledger pages, asking her to look at them purely as a professional, off the insurer's record.
3. Anna studies the entries and confirms, cautiously, that the sums look inflated relative to comparable period pieces — more than rounding or correction could explain.
4. She notices something about the signature itself, an unevenness suggesting it sits over an erasure, but stops short of saying the word forgery aloud.
5. Julian asks her to keep this between them for now, and she agrees, telling herself it's simply professional discretion, not yet seeing how far this will pull her in.
6. Neither mentions the other secret each is quietly keeping — Julian his marriage, Anna her family research.

--- SCENE 5 ---
1. Back at the house, Simon watches from his workshop doorway as Anna crosses the courtyard, freshly returned from her meeting with Julian.
2. He notices the focused, troubled look on her face and wonders, briefly, if she's closer to the truth than she realizes.
3. He considers, not for the first time, simply telling her who he is, and again talks himself out of it.
4. Anna stops by the workshop to ask him a small technical question about a restoration mark on one of the porcelain pieces, unaware of what she's really asking.
5. Simon answers her plainly and kindly, giving her real, useful information while giving away nothing of himself.
6. The audience feels the weight of what he almost says every time she speaks to him.

--- SCENE 6 ---
1. That night, alone in his workshop, Simon takes out the correspondence and ledger pages he's kept hidden for decades.
2. He reads his father's old letters again, written when Lin Wei-Chen still signed the ledgers as a full partner.
3. He sets a faded photograph of his father beside one he's taken himself of Anna since her arrival, studying the resemblance he's kept quiet about.
4. He allows himself the private fear that events are moving faster than he expected, that he may be running out of time to control how the truth comes out.
5. He puts everything away again before anyone can see, exactly as he has for fifty years.
6. The audience understands he is no longer simply keeping a secret — he is now racing a clock he can't see.

--- SCENE 7 ---
1. Marcus Tang meets with his staff to review the state of the Vantry family's mortgage debt and the shell companies handling its acquisition.
2. He gives the formal order to move from quiet interest to active control, directing them to begin acquiring the remaining debt outright.
3. His staff confirms the first debt payment call-in has already gone out through one of the shell companies, timed to leave no visible link to Marcus.
4. Marcus reminds his people that none of this can be traced back to him or Meridian Auctions before he's ready to reveal his hand.
5. He reflects privately that his grandfather Wing-Ho Tang never got the chance to prove what he suspected, and that he intends to finish what the old man started.
6. He has no idea the debt call-in he just ordered is the same one that pushed Julian into a loan Nora doesn't know about.

--- SCENE 8 ---
1. Marguerite finds Julian later that evening and mentions, lightly, that Marcus Tang has requested a second meeting.
2. She frames it as ordinary business interest, the same tone she used at dinner, careful to sound unbothered.
3. Julian, still preoccupied with the ledger, gives it only half his attention, assuring her it's nothing worth worrying about.
4. Marguerite doesn't correct him, though the request has unsettled her more than she's willing to admit even to her own son.
5. Neither realizes how much the other is quietly managing alone — Marguerite her unease about Marcus, Julian his fear about the ledgers.
6. The audience watches two people standing close together while each carries a separate, growing danger neither will name.

--- SCENE 9 ---
1. Julian comes home late again, this time from the pier meeting with Anna, and finds Nora waiting up.
2. She asks him directly where he's been, and he gives a half-answer about paperwork and the appraisal — true in fragments, missing what matters.
3. Nora notices he doesn't mention Anna Lin by name, though he easily could have, and the omission sits wrong with her.
4. She still doesn't produce the hotel receipt or voice her suspicion outright, watching him instead for something she can't quite name.
5. Julian, relieved she doesn't press further, tells himself again he's protecting her by saying nothing, not seeing how the silence itself is becoming the problem.
6. Nora decides, quietly, that she needs to see Marguerite soon and say some of what she's been carrying alone.

--- SCENE 10 ---
1. Alone in her guest room that night, Anna lays her official appraisal notes beside the private notebook where she's recorded fragments of her own family research.
2. Working through the ledger copies Julian gave her, she notices faint marks at the edge of several altered entries — traces of an erasure beneath the current signature.
3. She cross-references the affected lot numbers against the original 1938 partnership paperwork and finds each one tied to entries that once required two signatures, not one.
4. She doesn't yet let herself conclude forgery outright, but professionally she's now certain the alterations are deliberate, not clerical drift.
5. She glances, without particular weight, at the name Lin Wei-Chen already sitting in her private notebook from her own family research, not registering any link to the ledger work in front of her.
6. The audience, holding what Anna doesn't yet know, watches her sit one inference away from the truth of her own family.

--- SCENE 11 ---
1. The house settles for the night with three separate investigations now quietly underway under one roof — Nora and Julian's, Anna's professional one, and Simon's fifty-year-old private one — none yet aware how close the others run.
2. Marcus Tang's people complete the day's first move against the Vantry mortgage debt, unnoticed by anyone in the house.
3. Marguerite sleeps unaware that the story she still tells so proudly is now being quietly tested from three directions at once.
4. Nora resolves to speak to Marguerite soon, still not knowing her worry about Julian and her worry about the ledgers may be the same worry wearing two faces.
5. Simon extinguishes his workshop light last, the photograph and the correspondence locked away again, the fear that he's running out of time following him up the stairs.
6. The audience is left holding the full shape of what's converging — investigation, inheritance, and threat — while every character below still sees only their own single thread.
