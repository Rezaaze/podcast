--- SCENE 1 ---
1. It is the next morning, roughly twelve hours after episode two's close, and the household gathers for breakfast still nursing yesterday's separate unspoken worries.
2. Marguerite arrives distracted, having spent the night circling Marcus's request for a second meeting, unable to shake her unease.
3. Julian is quiet and guarded, replaying Nora's ledger findings and his meeting with Anna, careful to let nothing show.
4. Nora watches Julian's guardedness with the receipt still folded away in her drawer, no closer to deciding what to do with it.
5. No one raises Marcus, the ledger, or the receipt aloud; the meal passes in careful, deliberate ordinariness.
6. The audience recognizes each silence as load-bearing, each person now managing a different piece of the same widening problem.

--- SCENE 2 ---
1. Later that morning, Marguerite calls a brief household meeting and announces restricted access to the archive, citing the insurer's re-appraisal and standard security protocol.
2. She specifies that certain ledger cabinets will now require her personal sign-off before anyone opens them, including Nora and Anna.
3. Nora accepts the explanation on its surface but privately notes the timing feels sudden given her own recent digging.
4. Anna, present for the announcement, finds the justification thin but says nothing, unwilling to jeopardize her standing this early.
5. Marguerite tells herself this is prudent caution, not admission of anything, though her hands are unsteady as she locks the first cabinet herself.
6. The audience recognizes this as the first outward crack in the story Marguerite has always told about the collection's clean history.

--- SCENE 3 ---
1. Once the others disperse, Marguerite corners Julian privately and asks, without preamble, what exactly he and Nora found in the ledgers.
2. Julian downplays it, calling it a minor discrepancy not worth alarming anyone over, careful to keep Anna's name out of it entirely.
3. Marguerite presses harder than her tone lets on, and Julian notices for the first time how tightly controlled her questions are.
4. He begins to sense, watching her, that her sudden protocol and her pointed questions are about more than routine caution.
5. Neither admits what they're actually protecting the other from, and the conversation ends with both feeling less safe than before it started.
6. The audience sees the exact moment Julian's suspicion of his mother begins, even as he keeps hiding from her in the same breath.

--- SCENE 4 ---
1. That afternoon, searching Julian's desk for a household bill, Nora instead finds a bank letter confirming a personal loan taken out against their home.
2. The loan is dated to the same week Julian became distant and evasive, and she has no memory of ever signing or discussing it.
3. She sets the letter beside the hotel receipt she's kept hidden since episode one, the two documents now reinforcing rather than explaining each other.
4. She reads secrecy, another woman, and money moving without her knowledge as pieces of a single, worsening picture.
5. She still doesn't confront Julian, folding the letter away with the receipt instead, her fear hardening into something closer to certainty.
6. The audience, knowing the loan's true cause was Marcus's shell-company debt call-in, feels the gap between Nora's conclusion and the truth widen further.

--- SCENE 5 ---
1. That evening, Nora finally goes to Marguerite, done carrying the weight of the receipt and the loan letter alone.
2. She says she believes Julian may be having an affair with Anna Lin, laying out the evasions, the receipt, and the loan as evidence.
3. She stops short of mentioning the ledger discovery, treating it as a separate, unrelated worry rather than connecting the two.
4. Marguerite listens carefully, privately finding the affair theory plausible given how strange Julian has seemed lately, though she has no real evidence either way.
5. Marguerite promises to say nothing to Julian, treating the confidence as Nora's alone to share, and keeps her word even as it costs her something to hold.
6. The audience recognizes Nora has just handed Marguerite the one piece that could, if reframed, point toward the truth — and neither woman sees it yet.

--- SCENE 6 ---
1. In his workshop that same evening, Simon finds Anna lingering after hours, unsettled by the new archive restrictions and needing someone to talk to.
2. She admits, carefully, that she trusts him more than the short time she's known him should account for, without knowing why.
3. Simon feels the pull to tell her everything — who her great-grandfather was, who he is to her — rise further than it ever has before.
4. He starts to speak, gets as far as mentioning that he knew people connected to the collection's early years, and then stops himself.
5. He redirects the conversation back to safer, professional ground, watching a chance close that he isn't sure will come again.
6. The audience feels the weight of this near-miss, knowing how little distance now separates Simon's silence from the truth Anna is circling.

--- SCENE 7 ---
1. Across the harbor, Marcus meets his staff to confirm that every minority stake and the last of the mortgage debt are now fully under his control.
2. He reviews his partial copy of the 1951 ledger inconsistencies once more, deciding it's enough to apply pressure even without the full picture.
3. He instructs his staff to prepare a second, firmer approach to Marguerite, one that implies knowledge without yet naming it outright.
4. He reflects again on his grandfather Wing-Ho Tang's lost 1952 bid, treating this next move as the payoff of two years of careful positioning.
5. He has no sense of how close Nora and Anna's independent findings already run to his own evidence, believing himself the only one circling the truth.

--- SCENE 8 ---
1. Marcus secures a second private meeting with Marguerite, this time at the auction house rather than neutral ground.
2. He renews his offer to acquire the house, then adds a pointed remark that the family's history is more complicated than its public story.
3. He stops short of naming the ledgers or Lin Wei-Chen directly, letting the implication do the work instead.
4. Marguerite keeps her composure in front of him, but the hint lands exactly as he intended, shaking her far more than her expression shows.
5. She tells herself afterward that he's likely bluffing or exaggerating, unwilling to accept that he might hold something real.
6. The audience, knowing the true depth of what Marcus has and hasn't pieced together, watches Marguerite underestimate a threat that is only partly informed but still dangerous.

--- SCENE 9 ---
1. Shaken by Marcus's hint, Marguerite returns to the archive that night and expands the morning's restrictions, personally relocking every cabinet touching the 1951 ledgers.
2. She tells herself she's protecting the house from scrutiny, not admitting even to herself that she's protecting it from a truth she can't yet name.
3. She does not mention Marcus's specific hint to Julian or Nora, keeping it exactly as private as she kept Nora's earlier confidence.
4. Unknown to Marguerite, the newly tightened restrictions bring the cabinets nearer the shelf where Simon has kept his own hidden correspondence and ledger pages for decades.
5. The audience recognizes the danger Marguerite has just created without knowing it — her attempt to bury the truth may instead be the thing that exposes it.

--- SCENE 10 ---
1. The next day, Anna finds her archive access reduced even further, several cabinets now closed to her entirely despite her insurer mandate.
2. She raises it once, carefully, with Marguerite, and receives only the same thin insurance-protocol explanation as before.
3. Professionally, Anna concludes the family itself is hiding something structural about the collection's history, not just being cautious.
4. She has no way yet to connect this suspicion to Simon, to her own family's name already sitting in her private notebook, or to the ledger pages Julian showed her.
5. She decides to keep working quietly around the restrictions rather than escalate through the insurer, unwilling to jeopardize the trust she's built with Simon and the house.
6. The audience watches Anna stand one step closer to the truth than she realizes, still missing the one connection that would make everything else make sense.

--- SCENE 11 ---
1. That night, Simon locks his correspondence and ledger pages away again, aware now that Marguerite's tightened restrictions have brought them closer to discovery than ever before.
2. He allows himself, briefly, the same fear as the night before — that events are outrunning his fifty years of careful silence.
3. Marguerite sits alone with the memory of Marcus's hint, unable to share her fear with anyone in her own house, having asked the same silence of Nora just a day earlier.
4. Nora lies awake beside Julian, the receipt and the loan letter locked away, no closer to a confrontation than she was the night before.
5. Marcus closes out his day satisfied that his hint has landed, still unaware of how much closer Nora, Julian, and Anna's separate threads already run to what he only partly knows.
6. The audience is left holding the full converging shape of the season — archive doors closing around a hidden truth, a marriage misreading its own evidence, and a granddaughter and grandfather standing nearer to each other than either can yet say.
