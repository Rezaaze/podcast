--- PART 1 ---

[HOST | style: warm, inviting]
Welcome to The Rainy Night Tea House — Mandarin Mysteries, the podcast where you learn Chinese by solving a real mystery, one rainy evening at a time. This is not a textbook. This is a detective radio drama, and you are about to become part of the investigation.

Here's how every episode works. First, I'll set the scene in English, so you always know what's happening before the Chinese begins. After that, we slow everything down together in the Analysis, breaking apart the key sentences word by word. [HOST | style: storytelling, atmospheric]
Picture a small tea house on a narrow street in Chengdu. Rain taps steadily on the windows, the kind of rain that has fallen for three nights in a row now. Inside, the air smells of jasmine tea and wet stone. In the back room, mahjong tiles click against each other, tile against tile, a rhythm as familiar as a heartbeat.

The tea house belongs to Lin Qiu — 林秋 — a woman in her fifties with sharp eyes and an even sharper memory. She used to be a police detective. Now she pours tea instead of asking suspects questions. Or so she tells herself.

Every evening, an old man named Master Wen comes to her tea house. He is a watchmaker — someone who repairs clocks and watches for a living — and every night he sits by the same window, drinks the same tea, and talks about nothing in particular.

Except tonight, his seat by the window is empty. And it has been empty for three nights.

[HOST | style: curious, building tension]
Let's learn a few words before we step into the drama, because tonight, these words matter more than usual.

First: 不见了 — bú jiàn le. Literally, "not see — completed." It means something or someone has disappeared, has gone missing. If your keys are 不见了, you cannot find them anywhere. Tonight, it's Master Wen who is 不见了.

Second: 担心 — dān xīn — to worry. When someone you care about doesn't come home, you 担心 — you worry about them.

Third: 钟 — zhōng — clock. Not a small wristwatch, but a clock — the kind that sits on a table or hangs on a wall, ticking away the hours.

And finally, a strange one: 倒着走 — dào zhe zǒu — to walk backwards, to run in reverse. We usually use this for people walking backwards. But tonight... you'll hear it used for something that should never move backwards at all: a clock.

[HOST | style: hushed, drawing listener in]
Hold onto that phrase — 倒着走 — because it's about to become very, very strange indeed.

The door of the tea house is about to burst open. And what walks through it is not Master Wen.

It's his daughter. Soaked from the rain. Shaking. And carrying a story that doesn't make any sense — not yet.

Let's go inside.

[HOST | style: warm, inviting]
Let's slow everything down. Xiao Wen burst into the tea house soaked and terrified, and we heard some powerful Mandarin along the way. Let's isolate the sentences that matter most.

[HOST | style: clear, teacherly]
First, the sentence that started it all. Xiao Wen said her father was missing. Listen again, slowly.

[XIAO_WEN | style: trembling, slow | speed: 0.8]
我爸爸不见了。

[HOST | style: warm]
我爸爸不见了 — "My dad is gone." 不见了 literally means "cannot be seen anymore" — 不见 plus 了. That 了 is doing real work here: it marks a change, a completed shift from "here" to "not here." Say it with me, slowly.

[XIAO_WEN | style: slow, steady | speed: 0.8]
我爸爸不见了。

[HOST | style: curious]
Now the second sentence — the object at the center of our mystery. What did Xiao Wen find on the table?

[XIAO_WEN | style: slow, clear | speed: 0.8]
桌子上有一个钟。

[HOST | style: teacherly]
桌子上有一个钟 — "There is a clock on the table." 桌子上 means "on the table," and 有 simply means "there is/are." Simple structure, but notice — no 了 here. Why? Because this isn't describing a completed action, it's describing a current state: the clock is still there right now.

[HOST | style: building intrigue]
And here's the detail that turned a tea house chat into a real mystery.

[XIAO_WEN | style: slow, unsettled | speed: 0.8]
它倒着走。

[HOST | style: amazed]
它倒着走 — "It runs backwards." 倒着 means "in reverse, backwards," and 走 here means "to move, to go" — we use it for clocks the way English says a clock "runs." Put together: the clock moves backwards. Say it once more with me.

[XIAO_WEN | style: slow | speed: 0.8]
它倒着走。

[HOST | style: shifting to grammar mode]
Now, our grammar spotlight: 了 for completed events. This little word is everywhere in this episode, and it's doing more than one job, so let's untangle it.

[HOST | style: explaining]
When 了 attaches right after a verb, it marks that the action was completed. Remember when Lin Qiu asked about the light?

[LIN_QIU | style: sharp, noting detail | speed: 0.8]
灯已经开着了？

[HOST | style: explaining]
And Xiao Wen's answer:

[XIAO_WEN | style: recalling | speed: 0.8]
开了。

[HOST | style: teacherly]
开了 — "[it] was turned on" — completed action, done before Xiao Wen even arrived. That's 了 marking a finished event. Compare that to 不见了 from before — same 了, but there it marks a change of state: from present to absent. Two different jobs, one small word: completed action, or changed state. Context tells you which.

[HOST | style: reviewing]
Let's also review four important words from this scene, starting with fear itself.

[XIAO_WEN | style: trembling | speed: 0.8]
是的，往后走。

[HOST | style: teacherly]
Remember how shaken Xiao Wen was the whole time? That's 发抖 — fā dǒu — to tremble, to shake, whether from fear or from cold. Picture her hands shaking as she described the clock.

[HOST | style: teacherly]
Next, a pattern for something truly unprecedented: 从来没有...过 — cónglái méiyǒu...guò. This means "never has [done]" — 过 marks a past experience, and 从来没有 makes it absolute: never, not once. If Xiao Wen had said "我从来没有看过这样的钟," she'd mean "I have never seen a clock like this before" — a first-time, impossible experience.

[HOST | style: teacherly]
Third: 痕迹 — hén jì — a trace, a mark, a sign that something happened. Lin Qiu asked if there were any 痕迹 in the workshop — footprints, disturbances, evidence.

[LIN_QIU | style: thoughtful | speed: 0.8]
可是没有别的痕迹了。

[HOST | style: teacherly]
And finally: 隐瞒 — yǐn mán — to conceal, to hide the truth. Xiao Wen felt Old Shen was 隐瞒 something on the phone.

[XIAO_WEN | style: uneasy | speed: 0.8]
好像在隐瞒什么。

[HOST | style: warm, wrapping up]
不见了, 了 for completion, 发抖, 从来没有...过, 痕迹, 隐瞒 — six pieces of the puzzle, all pointing toward one very suspicious business partner. Let's head back to the tea house for the recap.
