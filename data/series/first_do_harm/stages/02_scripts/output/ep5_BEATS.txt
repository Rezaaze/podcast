--- SCENE 1 ---
1. Moorhouse's composure returns almost instantly, and he turns back to Ada as though Wycliffe's confession hadn't landed at all.
2. Ada seizes the crack Wycliffe just opened and asks Moorhouse outright how many patients lie buried behind the ward wall.
3. Moorhouse doesn't deny it, answering only that it's "enough to matter, and not enough to stop," giving the first number-shaped confirmation of the burials any character has voiced aloud.
4. He sets his pen down with visible boredom, treating the admission as paperwork rather than confession, refusing Ada the reaction of guilt or fear she needed from him.
5. Wycliffe watches Moorhouse's calm and privately realizes his tip to the board may not be enough to stop whatever Moorhouse intends to do next.
6. Ada understands that Moorhouse's composure means he already has a plan for surviving discovery, and that frightens her more than the confession itself.

--- SCENE 2 ---
1. Moorhouse leads Ada into the operating theatre, using the surrounding instruments as props for self-justification rather than as evidence against him.
2. He tells her that medicine has always demanded sacrifices no one wants named, folding the buried women into a tradition of necessary, uncredited loss.
3. He says history will remember his results long after her name and her testimony are forgotten, masking any private fear behind theatrical certainty.
4. Ada recognizes this isn't performance for her benefit but his genuine belief, revealing to the audience that his composure has never been an act.
5. She hides how frightened she still is, keeping her voice steady, sensing that visible terror is exactly what would confirm to him that he's already won.
6. The audience, already aware Wycliffe went outside the clinic, watches Moorhouse rehearse his own legacy without knowing exposure is already moving against him.

--- SCENE 3 ---
1. A letter from the inspector arrives at the ward, worded to ask only about "irregularities," with neither doctor yet explaining to the other why it's phrased so narrowly.
2. Wycliffe reads it and hides his relief that his own softened report kept the word "murder" out of it, still hoping to negotiate his own immunity when the audit comes.
3. Moorhouse reads the same letter and privately recognizes that a complaint about irregularities is one he can survive, unlike an accusation of killing.
4. He signs the audit request with a steady hand, giving neither Ada nor Wycliffe any visible sign of the fear a wider inquiry would have provoked in him.
5. Moorhouse begins silently rehearsing how he'll reshape the coming audit's findings before anyone connects the ledger to the disturbed ground behind the wall, a plan he shares with no one.
6. The audience learns, ahead of Ada, that both doctors are quietly relieved by the letter for opposite self-serving reasons, and that the truth of the deaths remains hidden from the inspector for now.
