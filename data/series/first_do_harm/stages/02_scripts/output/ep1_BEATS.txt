--- SCENE 1 ---
1. Ada enters the theatre straight from a procedure, ether still on her, and drops Mrs. Hale's consent form on Wycliffe's tray instead of handing it to him.
2. She states flatly that the handwriting isn't Mrs. Hale's, citing six weeks of dressing her wounds as proof she knows the hand.
3. Wycliffe's wipe of the scalpel stops mid-motion, a physical tell that lands before he says a word.
4. The audience registers his stillness as guilt, though Ada reads it only as surprise or offense.
5. Ada presses for an answer, unaware she has just named — without knowing it — the exact fraud Wycliffe committed on Moorhouse's instruction.
6. Wycliffe says nothing yet, buying a beat of silence that the audience feels as evasion, not confusion.

--- SCENE 2 ---
1. Wycliffe recovers enough to offer an excuse — a clerical mix-up, or his own hand unsteady from fever that week — but the explanation comes out thin and poorly ordered.
2. Ada doesn't back down, and her continued stare visibly rattles him further, close to cracking the arrangement he's hidden even from himself as routine.
3. Moorhouse enters at exactly this pressure point, before Wycliffe can either confess or convincingly lie.
4. He praises Ada's diligence with warmth that reads as one degree too calibrated, unsettling rather than reassuring.
5. Under cover of that praise, he takes physical possession of the consent form, removing the only evidence from the room before Ada can secure it elsewhere.
6. He promises a full explanation "in good time," a delay tactic Ada accepts at face value but the audience recognizes as containment — Wycliffe, meanwhile, understands it as Moorhouse protecting him specifically, deepening his debt of loyalty.

--- SCENE 3 ---
1. That night, Ada lets herself into Moorhouse's unlocked study, already suspicious enough to go looking rather than wait for his promised explanation.
2. She pulls a second discharge file unrelated to Mrs. Hale, searching for a pattern rather than a single anomaly.
3. She finds the same distinctive looping hand on a signature dated eight months earlier, proving to her this is not an isolated incident.
4. This is the moment she moves — in her own understanding — from "one forged signature" to "how many," though she still assumes clerical fraud, not death.
5. The audience, holding the full case file, feels the gap between her question and the buried truth behind the ward wall that the word "how many" is actually asking.
6. Before she can finish voicing the thought, the study door creaks open behind her, ending the scene on the exposure she cannot yet see coming.
