--- SCENE 1 ---
1. A night-shift worker at the Old Textile Mill hears something wrong and finds a body staged in a deliberate tableau among the old machinery.
2. Pinned above the body is a note, sealed, addressed by name to Detective Marcus Reid rather than to the department.
3. The worker's shock centers on the strangeness of the staging, not just the death — the arrangement feels composed, not chaotic.
4. The audience sees the personal address on the note before any character in the precinct does — the first gap between what we know and what Reid's world knows.
5. Uniformed officers arrive and immediately flag the note as unusual, triggering the call up the chain to Reid.

--- SCENE 2 ---
1. Reid and Park examine the note together at his desk; Park reads it as addressed to the department's lead detective, a professional courtesy, nothing more.
2. Reid recognizes his own name and a reference to his wedding anniversary date, and privately understands this is not procedural — it is personal.
3. Reid says nothing to Park about the personal nature of the note, letting her keep reading it as case correspondence.
4. Captain Hollis arrives to brief them on early mayor's office pressure, treating this as a high-profile but ordinary homicide to close fast.
5. Reid lets Hollis's ordinary-homicide framing stand uncorrected, quietly deciding not to mention the letter names him specifically, afraid of being pulled from the case.
6. The audience learns Reid is already choosing concealment over disclosure, before anyone else in the room has reason to suspect it.

--- SCENE 3 ---
1. Mia sets the table for their anniversary dinner, checking the clock more than she checks the food.
2. She texts Reid a light, unworried reminder, assuming this is like any other case-night delay he's weathered before.
3. Minutes stretch into an hour; she starts a private note on her phone — the time, the missed call, small details she's begun quietly logging.
4. She rationalizes the silence to herself, telling herself this is simply another demanding case, not yet aware anything is different this time.
5. The audience sees the anniversary date land on Mia's calendar exactly as it appeared in the killer's note — a connection she cannot make and doesn't yet know exists.

--- SCENE 4 ---
1. The Curator moves through a private room of catalogued objects, each item arranged with the same deliberate order as the mill tableau.
2. He unlocks a drawer and adds a single new item to it, handling the ritual with unhurried, practiced care.
3. Nothing in his manner suggests urgency or guilt — this is maintenance of something long-standing, not a reaction to being caught.
4. The audience senses this is one entry in a much older collection, though nothing on screen confirms how old.
5. He allows himself a brief, private satisfaction — the first sign that tonight's event was not an isolated act but a planned continuation of something already underway.

--- SCENE 5 ---
1. The bullpen empties for the night; Reid stays at his desk, alone, laying out the cipher pieces by hand.
2. He works past the point of normal diligence, absorbed in a way that goes beyond simple casework.
3. He glances at his phone, sees a missed call from Mia, and sets it face-down without answering.
4. He tells himself he'll make it up to her once the case is closed, not registering that he has no idea what "closed" means for a case like this.
5. The audience watches him choose the cipher over his marriage in a small, almost unconscious gesture — the first physical proof of where his attention now lives.

--- SCENE 6 ---
1. Reid and Park sit over coffee at the diner, decompressing after the long shift in what should be an easy, familiar routine.
2. Park tries to talk through the case the way they always do, expecting Reid to loop her into his thinking as usual.
3. Reid answers late, or not at all, clearly turning the cipher over in his head instead of listening.
4. Park notices the gap but reads it as case-stress, not as Reid actively withholding something from her.
5. The audience recognizes Reid's distraction as the personal note eating at him, information Park does not have access to.

--- SCENE 7 ---
1. Reid comes home hours after the dinner was meant to start; the food is cold, the candles burned down.
2. Mia's greeting is brittle — not yet an accusation, but no longer simple relief that he's safe.
3. Reid apologizes in general terms about "the case," without mentioning the note is addressed to him or that it references their anniversary.
4. Mia accepts the apology on the surface but keeps a private doubt to herself, adding tonight to her growing mental ledger.
5. The audience holds two facts Mia doesn't: that the case knows their anniversary date, and that Reid knows it does.

--- SCENE 8 ---
1. A dead drop above the harbor at the Riverside Overlook yields a second cipher, found by a patrol sweep or passerby.
2. The location and method mirror the first note closely enough to confirm a single author, not a copycat.
3. Word reaches the precinct that the killer is not finished — this is a sequence, not an isolated event.
4. The audience recognizes the overlook as another deliberate placement, consistent with the Curator's long, unhurried method, though no character yet grasps the pattern's true age.
5. The stakes shift from "solve a murder" to "keep pace with an unfolding design," though only the audience currently frames it that way.

--- SCENE 9 ---
1. Hollis is briefed on the second cipher and immediately recalculates the case's visibility and risk.
2. The mayor's office pressure intensifies, with Hollis relaying pointed new questions about how fast this will be resolved.
3. Hollis pushes Reid for a timeline, still operating on the assumption this is an unusually elaborate but ultimately ordinary homicide.
4. Reid gives a controlled, reassuring answer that omits how personally targeted he now believes the case to be.
5. The audience sees Hollis's confidence in a normal resolution grow more misplaced with each new development.

--- SCENE 10 ---
1. Alone again in his study, the Curator handles an object or photograph connected to Reid's private life — something no public record would contain.
2. His attention to the detail is unhurried and familiar, the ease of someone revisiting material he already knows intimately.
3. A small gesture or arrangement implies observation stretching back further than the current case, not a recent surveillance effort.
4. Nothing overt states his intentions, but the moment signals a level of study far beyond simple stalking of a case detective.
5. The audience is left certain the Curator's knowledge of Reid predates the first body by a significant margin, information no one in the precinct possesses.

--- SCENE 11 ---
1. Mia confronts the pattern rather than the single night — missed dinner, missed calls, absence stacking up across the season.
2. She states plainly that this doesn't feel like the other hard cases they've survived before; something in him feels different this time.
3. She asks him directly what's actually going on, wanting more than a vague case-stress answer.
4. Reid deflects, giving a partial truth about pressure and workload while withholding the personal nature of the letters entirely.
5. Mia senses the deflection but can't yet name what's missing, leaving the conversation unresolved rather than settled.
6. The audience recognizes this as the moment Mia's fear of distance begins hardening into suspicion, even though the true cause is the case, not infidelity.

--- SCENE 12 ---
1. Park offers to take on more of the caseload, sensing Reid is stretched thin and wanting to help the way partners do.
2. Reid, distracted mid-thought on the cipher, brushes off the offer more sharply than he intends.
3. Park's expression shows the sting of being shut out, though she doesn't yet suspect deliberate concealment.
4. Reid catches his own tone a beat too late, offering a half-hearted correction that doesn't fully repair it.
5. The audience sees Park's earlier assumption — that Reid loops her into everything — quietly starting to erode.

--- SCENE 13 ---
1. Reid returns alone to the Old Textile Mill at night, before any other officers arrive for the day.
2. He circles the tableau site repeatedly, studying details with a focus that goes past professional thoroughness.
3. His behavior reads as compulsive rather than procedural — he is drawn back, not simply doing due diligence.
4. He touches or lingers over an element of the staging in a way that suggests private fascination, not just investigation.
5. The audience watches the case begin to consume him physically as well as mentally, alone and unwitnessed by anyone who could flag the change.

--- SCENE 14 ---
1. Sitting alone in a back booth at the diner, Reid is approached by a passerby who presses a new note into his hand.
2. The exchange happens too fast and at the wrong angle for Reid to get a clear look at the person's face.
3. By the time Reid reacts and turns, the passerby is already gone, folded into the ordinary flow of the diner.
4. Reid opens the note privately, without calling for backup or informing anyone else in the moment.
5. The audience registers this as proof of the killer's confidence and reach — delivering directly, in public, without fear of capture.

--- SCENE 15 ---
1. Back at the precinct, Reid sits alone with the new note and works through its details methodically.
2. He isolates the encoded date and decodes it fully, only to realize it matches his and Mia's wedding anniversary exactly.
3. The chill of realization lands: this cannot be a lucky guess or public record — it is proof of long, personal observation.
4. He understands, for the first time, that the killer's knowledge predates the case itself, undercutting his belief that he was chosen at random as lead detective.
5. He still does not tell anyone — not Hollis, not Park, not Mia — keeping the discovery, and its weight, entirely to himself.
6. The audience closes the episode holding the full shape of what Reid now senses only partially: that he was studied and chosen long before the first body ever surfaced.
