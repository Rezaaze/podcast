--- SCENE 1 ---
1. Forensics reports a torn receipt bearing Daniel Chen's thumbprint recovered near the dump site.
2. The team treats this as their first real physical lead after two ciphers and no suspect.
3. Reid privately notes the evidence is thin — procedural cause, not proof — but voices only cautious agreement.
4. Hollis authorizes moving fast, framing this as the break the mayor's office has been waiting for.
5. The audience knows what the room doesn't: this print was planted, engineered by the real killer to be found.
6. Reid hides his private doubt about Daniel fitting the killer's pattern, letting the momentum carry forward uncorrected.

--- SCENE 2 ---
1. Daniel Chen is brought into interrogation, confused and frightened, unable to understand why he's there.
2. He answers questions honestly, offering an account of being home alone that has no witness to confirm it.
3. Detectives read his lack of alibi as suspicious rather than simply unlucky.
4. Daniel mentions in passing having once worked at a laundromat, a detail no one in the room yet connects to anything.
5. The audience recognizes this offhand detail as a thread to the Curator's known pattern, invisible to the detectives present.
6. Daniel's bewilderment plays as the genuine confusion of someone with no idea why he was chosen.

--- SCENE 3 ---
1. Joy Chen arrives at the courthouse to find reporters already gathered, having heard of her brother's arrest before she could reach her.
2. She's caught off guard, thrust into the spotlight without preparation.
3. She knows Daniel has no history of violence and was simply home that night, and says so on instinct.
4. She doesn't yet grasp that the case against him rests on a single piece of evidence, though she can feel it's flimsy.
5. She assumes, wrongly, that the department chose Daniel out of laziness or prejudice, not realizing he was deliberately engineered as the fall guy.
6. The audience watches her righteous anger form around a truth she doesn't fully possess yet.

--- SCENE 4 ---
1. Word of Daniel Chen's arrest reaches the Curator through an ordinary channel — a news alert, a radio mention.
2. He receives the news with unreadable calm, no flicker of alarm or urgency.
3. He continues whatever small, deliberate task he was already engaged in, unbothered.
4. A private, contained satisfaction crosses him — the arrest is proceeding exactly as intended.
5. The audience understands what no one in the precinct does: the Curator chose Daniel precisely for this outcome, to buy time and study the department's response.
6. Nothing in the scene confirms guilt outright to an outside observer, preserving his ambiguity to anyone watching him from outside the story.

--- SCENE 5 ---
1. Hollis meets Reid in the bullpen, relieved the arrest has eased pressure from the mayor's office.
2. He pushes Reid to move toward closing the case quickly while the department has the advantage.
3. Reid gives a measured, agreeable response, not revealing his private uncertainty about Daniel.
4. Hollis remains confident this is a normal, if high-profile, homicide heading toward resolution.
5. The audience senses the gap between Hollis's confidence and the truth widening further with each scene.
6. Reid's silence here continues the season's pattern: choosing ease over disclosure.

--- SCENE 6 ---
1. A new cipher is discovered at the Riverside Overlook, despite Daniel Chen sitting in custody.
2. The handwriting and pattern match the previous notes exactly, ruling out a copycat.
3. Reid is the first to register what this means: the real killer is either unaware of the arrest or entirely unconcerned by it.
4. He says nothing to the officers around him, keeping his dawning doubt private.
5. The audience already knows, from the Curator's perspective, that the arrest was planned around exactly this outcome.
6. Reid's confidence in the Chen case begins to crack, though he has not yet admitted this to himself fully.

--- SCENE 7 ---
1. Reid and Park sit down at the diner, ostensibly to review routine case updates.
2. Park raises the timeline inconsistency in Daniel's account, cautious but direct.
3. Reid listens more closely than he has in recent scenes, the new cipher weighing on him unspoken.
4. Park doesn't yet know about the personal addressing of the letters or the new overlook cipher's implications; she reads Reid's engagement as simple diligence.
5. Reid stops short of admitting his doubts fully, still withholding the personal note and his fear that Daniel is the wrong man.
6. The audience watches Park's doubts crystallize in real time while Reid's remain carefully unspoken.

--- SCENE 8 ---
1. Reid comes home distracted, his mind still on the Riverside Overlook cipher rather than present with Mia.
2. Mia notices he's more absent than usual, even measured against a season of missed dinners and late nights.
3. She makes a small effort at connection — a shared meal, a simple question — that goes mostly unanswered.
4. Reid offers only surface responses, his attention visibly elsewhere.
5. Mia says nothing accusatory yet, but the evening passes in near-silence, another entry in her private ledger.
6. The audience recognizes this as the marriage eroding under the weight of a secret Mia still doesn't know exists.

--- SCENE 9 ---
1. Back at his desk, Reid turns over the timeline discrepancies Park raised, alone with his growing unease.
2. He privately concludes Daniel doesn't fit the killer's pattern — too clean, too convenient, too vulnerable.
3. He recognizes that voicing this now would undo the arrest and pull scrutiny back onto him and the unresolved case.
4. He chooses to stay quiet, letting the department's relief stand uncorrected.
5. The audience sees this as Reid's clearest act of concealment yet — not just about the letters, but about the truth of an innocent man's situation.
6. His wrongful belief that pursuing Daniel might provoke the real killer into a mistake keeps him from acting, though the audience knows it provokes nothing.

--- SCENE 10 ---
1. Joy Chen, out of options and out of patience, calls an impromptu press conference on the courthouse steps.
2. She declares her brother's innocence plainly, citing his lack of any violent history.
3. She has no proof beyond her certainty, but her conviction is unwavering and public.
4. Reporters press her for details she doesn't have, and she holds her ground regardless.
5. The audience watches her stake a public claim that is, unknown to her, entirely correct.
6. Her defiance sets her on a collision course with a department that has already committed to its narrative.

--- SCENE 11 ---
1. Alone at the Old Textile Mill before other officers arrive, Reid lays the cipher timeline against Daniel's known movements.
2. The dates and windows don't align cleanly — Daniel's account doesn't fit the killer's precision.
3. Reid's compulsive focus on the scene deepens, the discrepancy feeding his private obsession rather than easing it.
4. He doesn't call this in or flag it officially, choosing instead to sit with the doubt himself.
5. The audience sees Reid's isolation becoming both a personal and professional liability, unwitnessed by anyone who might intervene.
6. His unwillingness to surface this finding continues to protect the fragile stability the arrest has bought him.

--- SCENE 12 ---
1. Park returns to interrogation on her own initiative, re-interviewing Daniel without waiting for direction from upstairs.
2. Daniel repeats his account, small inconsistencies surfacing under closer, more careful questioning.
3. Park recognizes these gaps as real, not incidental — the timeline genuinely doesn't hold up.
4. She knows the department doesn't want to hear this now that the arrest has calmed the mayor's office.
5. She hasn't yet raised her doubts directly with Reid, still assuming he'll catch the same problems himself.
6. The audience sees Park's suspicion sharpening into something closer to conviction, still isolated from Reid's parallel doubts.

--- SCENE 13 ---
1. Reid cancels another plan with Mia at the last minute, citing the case without elaborating.
2. Mia doesn't argue this time — she simply goes quiet, absorbing it as one more entry in the pattern.
3. The silence between them carries more weight than any argument would, each aware something has shifted.
4. Reid senses the change but still believes there will be a clean moment to fix things once the case resolves.
5. Mia's private assumption — that this is just a worse version of old patterns — holds, though the audience knows the real cause is the hidden case, not simple neglect.
6. The scene closes on a distance neither of them names aloud.

--- SCENE 14 ---
1. Hollis takes a sharper call from the mayor's liaison, the tone shifting from relief to warning.
2. The liaison flags the risk that a shaky arrest could backfire publicly if the evidence doesn't hold.
3. Hollis, still believing the case is otherwise ordinary, treats this as political pressure to manage rather than a sign the arrest itself is wrong.
4. He pushes back on Reid for reassurance, wanting confirmation the Chen case is solid.
5. Reid gives careful, noncommittal answers, still withholding both his doubts and the letters' personal nature.
6. The audience watches Hollis's exposure grow, unaware how close the "easy answer" is to collapsing.

--- SCENE 15 ---
1. A new note surfaces at the Riverside Overlook, timed and placed with the same deliberate precision as before.
2. Its content directly references the arrest, reading less like ignorance of it and more like open mockery.
3. Reid deciphers enough to understand the killer isn't just unconcerned — the note seems to taunt the department's choice.
4. This confirms, in Reid's mind, what he's suspected since Scene 9: they have the wrong man.
5. He still doesn't report this fully upward, keeping the weight of it to himself as the episode closes.
6. The audience ends the episode certain of what Reid now only fears: Daniel Chen is innocent, and the real Curator is still watching, unhurried and unafraid.
