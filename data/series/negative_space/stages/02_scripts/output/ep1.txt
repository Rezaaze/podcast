--- PART 1 ---

[NARRATOR]
Every photograph is a room you can't leave. Negative Space follows Ethan Voss, a digital forensics analyst who opens an anonymous hard drive expecting routine evidence and instead finds a stranger's shadow stitched into a dozen family portraits — every family now dead in accidents no one questioned closely enough. As he chases the metadata deeper than his job requires, the people closest to him are keeping their own quiet frames out of view: a girlfriend running her own investigation, a best friend protecting a mistake, a mentor who has spent years developing something far darker than film. This season, the picture only gets clearer once it's too late to look away.

[NARRATOR | style: low, unhurried]
Episode One. The Anonymous Drive.

[NARRATOR | style: measured, atmospheric]
At night, this city becomes a grid of lit rectangles. Kitchen windows. Bedroom windows. The blue flicker of a television left on for company. From above, it looks almost tender — thousands of small, warm rooms, each one certain of its own privacy.

[NARRATOR | style: measured, atmospheric]
No one checks who else might be looking back.

[NARRATOR | style: quiet, deliberate]
A photograph is supposed to be a kindness. A held moment, offered up freely — here is my kitchen, my child, my ordinary Tuesday. It asks nothing in return but a glance.

[NARRATOR | style: quiet, deliberate]
It rarely occurs to anyone that the moment might already have an audience. That somewhere just past the edge of the frame, close enough to be caught by accident, someone has been standing very still, waiting to be included.

[NARRATOR | style: low, steady]
This is a city where everyone believes they are the one doing the watching. The journalist trailing a story she won't name. The analyst reading a file he hasn't logged. The friend keeping score of who gets credited and who doesn't. Each of them certain they hold the vantage point. None of them checking what's behind their own shoulder.

[NARRATOR | style: low, steady]
Somewhere tonight, a package with no sender's name is already moving through the city toward a desk where someone will open it out of nothing more than curiosity.

[NARRATOR | style: hushed, final]
It will look, at first, like someone else's family album. It will take longer than anyone would like to admit before a single person in this story understands what they're actually holding — or how long they've already been standing in someone else's frame.
