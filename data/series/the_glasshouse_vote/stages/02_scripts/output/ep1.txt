--- PART 1 ---

[NARRATOR]
Above a century-old apartment block, a glasshouse cooperative has survived wars, blackouts, and three generations of tenants who tended it like family. Now a developer is offering more money than the building has ever seen — and all it takes to close the deal is one routine inspection. But every time the vote to sell comes up, something gets in the way: a lost signature, a scheduling conflict, a sudden objection from the chair. This is the story of seven people who share a roof, a vote, and nothing else they're willing to admit out loud — and of a greenhouse that has been quietly keeping their secrets for exactly as long as it's been keeping them warm.

[NARRATOR]
A Fortune on the Roof. It is early morning in the building's lobby — the season's first surprise, delivered before anyone has had their coffee.

[SFX: a glass door swinging open with a chime]

[IGGY_CHEN | style: brisk, professional, unaware he's speaking to no one who matters yet]

[NARRATOR]

[SFX: a pen scratching quickly on a clipboard]

[AIKO_TANAKA | style: mild curiosity, just passing through]

[NARRATOR]

[SFX: mailbox door clanking shut]

[AIKO_TANAKA | style: suddenly still, masking it fast]
Halloran. Huh.

[NARRATOR]
By midmorning, the word has outpaced the elevator. Liwei Castellan — the board chair's estranged son, recently back from a failed venture overseas — checks his mailbox and overhears two neighbors already trading a number that hasn't officially been spoken yet.

[SFX: a stack of mail sliding out of a metal box]

[LIWEI_CASTELLAN | style: trying to sound casual, failing]

[NARRATOR]
A resident, unbothered, just shrugs and repeats what she heard third-hand, already three times removed from anything true.

[LIWEI_CASTELLAN | style: quiet, almost to himself, a flush of hope he can't quite suppress]
That can't be real. That's not — that's not a real number for this building.

[NARRATOR]
He says it like a caution to himself. It doesn't land that way.

[SFX: elevator bell dinging]

[LIWEI_CASTELLAN | style: energized, forgetting to lower his voice]
If that's even close to true, this changes everything. This actually changes everything.

[NARRATOR]
He doesn't notice the doorman watching him, or the way the word "Halloran" is already moving through the building faster than any envelope could carry it — into a treasurer's office, under a workshop door, past a woman on the fourth floor who hasn't left her apartment yet today. No one standing in this lobby knows that a single sealed envelope is about to press on four people in four very different ways, or that the greenhouse upstairs has been waiting, patiently, for a morning exactly like this one.
