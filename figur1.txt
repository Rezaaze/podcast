--- PART 1 ---

Close your eyes and step into a world lit by gas flame and blue ether-vapor: a world of glass specimen jars, steel trays laid out like surgical altars, and the low hiss of instruments cooling in carbolic acid. Somewhere behind a locked door, a patient waits — trusting, terrified, utterly at the mercy of a man in a white coat who believes, with his whole soul, that he is about to save them. This is the anatomy theater of the possible, where the border between healer and butcher is drawn not in malice but in conviction. Over the next three episodes, we will walk the wards, the operating rooms, and the asylum corridors of physicians who set out to conquer pain, madness, and disease itself — and who, in their hunger for the cure, forgot to ask what it would cost the bodies beneath their hands. Their names are etched in medical textbooks. Their victims' names were, for a long time, erased. Tonight, we restore both to the record. Welcome to Anatomy of the Damned.

Montgomery, Alabama, 1845. The air outside is thick with cotton dust and the sour reek of the tanning yards. Inside a small wooden building behind Dr. James Marion Sims's home — a structure he calls his hospital, though it holds no license, no oversight, no board of inquiry — a woman named Anarcha Westcott lies on a table built for this purpose alone. She is seventeen years old. She has already survived a childbirth that lasted three days, a labor so catastrophic it tore a hole between her bladder and her vagina, a wound with a name most Americans have never heard: vesicovaginal fistula. Urine leaks from her body constantly now, an unstoppable, humiliating tide that soaks her clothing, blisters her thighs, and marks her, in the cruel arithmetic of the plantation, as damaged property. There is no anesthesia in this room tonight — ether will not reach American surgery for another year, and even after it does, Sims will choose, again and again, not to use it on women like her. There is only opium, sometimes, and the strength of four other enslaved women, brought in to hold Anarcha's arms and legs against the table.

Marion Sims stands over her with a bent spoon repurposed into a vaginal speculum, an instrument of his own invention, and he leans in close in the gray morning light to see what no physician before him has properly seen. He believes, sincerely, that what he is about to do will end suffering. He is not wrong about the disease. He is catastrophically wrong about the price he is willing to make someone else pay for its cure.

--- PART 2 ---

To understand how a twenty-nine-year-old country doctor ended up hunched over an enslaved teenager with a bent spoon in his hand, we have to go back further — to a young man who, by his own later admission, never wanted to practice medicine at all. James Marion Sims was born in 1813 in the Lancaster District of South Carolina, the son of a tavern keeper and militia captain who expected his boy to become a lawyer, a banker, anything but a doctor. Sims himself, in the memoir he would write decades later, called medicine "a repulsive trade" in his youth, and confessed he found the sight of a limb amputation so revolting during his student days at Jefferson Medical College in Philadelphia that he nearly abandoned the profession before he had truly begun it. He was, by his own account, an unremarkable student, more interested in horses and hunting than in anatomy plates. And yet something in him could not let failure stand. He graduated in 1835, drifted through a string of early practices in South Carolina, watched two of his first infant patients die within days of each other, and fled — first to Lancaster, then west to the raw frontier town of Montgomery, Alabama, where in 1840 he began building a modest general practice among planters, merchants, and their human property.

It was here, treating the ordinary sicknesses of a cotton boomtown, that Sims first encountered the condition that would define — and damn — his name. Vesicovaginal fistula was, in 1845, considered essentially incurable. Textbooks called it hopeless. Prominent surgeons in Europe had tried and abandoned the problem. Women who developed it, whether enslaved or free, were often quietly discarded by their communities, unable to bear the smell, the leakage, the shame that clung to them like a second skin. For enslaved women specifically, a fistula meant something colder still: diminished value, a body no longer reliably productive in the eyes of an owner, sometimes outright abandonment to a shed or a cabin at the field's edge. Sims saw in this abandonment an opportunity that no ambitious young surgeon in Philadelphia or Charleston had access to — patients nobody would miss, complaints nobody in power would investigate, and consent that, under slavery, could never truly exist at all. He did not frame it to himself this way. He framed it as providence, delivering him the very cases that would let him build something no one else had built. The bent spoon was only the beginning.

--- PART 3 ---

By late 1845, Sims had built the physical apparatus of his ambition: a small clapboard structure behind his house on Montgomery's Lapsley Street, fitted with eight rough beds and a single operating table, financed entirely out of his own growing practice. Alabama law gave enslaved people no legal standing to refuse a master's command, and slaveholders across the county soon understood what Sims was offering. A fistula in a bondswoman was, in the plain economic language of the time, a liability — a body that could no longer work in the fields, could not be sold, could not be trusted near a household without offending the delicate sensibilities of a planter's family. Sims proposed to board, feed, and treat these women at his own expense for as long as their treatment required, asking owners only to forgo the value of their labor in the meantime. In return, he received something no hospital in Philadelphia or Charleston could have supplied him: patients with no legal voice, no advocate, and no mechanism by which consent could ever meaningfully be given or withheld.

He called the structure his hospital. It functioned, in practice, as a slave quarters converted into a surgical theater, and its patient population was staffed largely by itself. Sims trained the enslaved women recovering from one operation to nurse, restrain, and assist during the next, teaching them to hold a fellow patient's shoulders, monitor her breathing, change her dressings, and observe her wounds for signs of infection — skills that made these women, in effect, the first surgical nurses of American gynecology, credited nowhere. It was during this period that Sims developed the positioning that still bears his name in operating rooms today: the patient on her left side, knees drawn toward the chest, a posture that let air pressure alone hold the vaginal walls open and gave him, for the first time in the history of the procedure, a clear and unobstructed view of the fistula itself.

Word of the work spread among Montgomery's small medical community, and for a time, other physicians came to observe. Sims recorded their presence proudly in his own later writings — until, one by one, they stopped coming. Some found the repeated, unanesthetized procedures more than they could countenance as spectators, even in an era that asked little sympathy for enslaved suffering. Within roughly a year, Sims noted that his professional audience had dwindled to almost nothing. He did not take this as a warning. He read it instead as evidence that lesser men lacked his resolve, and he continued the work with only his patients — bound to him by law, not choice — remaining in the room.

--- PART 4 ---

The first operation Sims performed for fistula, in 1845, was on a woman named Lucy, and the surgical record he later set down in his own hand describes it in terms that leave little room for euphemism. Lucy was young, likely still a teenager, and had developed her fistula after a difficult birth. Sims placed her in the position he had devised, opened the tissue with instruments he had shaped himself, and closed the wound using silk thread — a suture material that, as he would soon discover, the human body treated as a foreign invader, provoking inflammation and infection almost as reliably as it closed a wound. Before the procedure ended, Sims inserted a sponge and a small metal catheter into Lucy's bladder to divert urine away from the healing tissue, then sent her back to recover in the small ward behind his house.

What followed, by Sims's own account, was among the most severe complications of his early career. The catheter, whether from the metal itself or from an obstruction it created, triggered a violent inflammatory response. Lucy's abdomen swelled. She developed a raging fever and what Sims himself described as a state resembling metal poisoning, her body wracked with chills and delirium for days. She screamed, by his account, in an agony he acknowledged as extreme even by the standards of a surgery performed without anesthesia — screams audible, he wrote, well beyond the walls of the small building that housed her. For a period of time, her survival was genuinely in doubt. She did survive. She did not consent to that risk in any sense recognizable under the law of any era, and no inquest, no colleague, no institution stood positioned to ask whether she should have been asked at all.

Sims recorded the episode not as an indictment of his method but as a lesson in technique — evidence that silk thread and metal catheters required refinement, that the problem was material, not moral. Lucy recovered enough, within weeks, to resume the very labor her body had betrayed her into losing in the first place: assisting in the recovery of the next woman brought to that table. And it was in the aftermath of Lucy's near-death that Sims turned his full and undivided attention to the patient who would occupy that operating table more than any other human being in American surgical history — the seventeen-year-old girl he had first examined with a bent spoon, Anarcha Westcott, whose body was about to become the proving ground for an obsession that would consume the next four years of his life.

--- PART 5 ---

Anarcha did not come to that table once. She came back thirty times.

Between 1845 and 1849, in that small clapboard ward on Lapsley Street, Sims operated on her again and again — each attempt a slight variation on the last, each failure recorded in his own hand as data, not devastation. The fistula would seem to close. Then, within days, the silk sutures he trusted would begin to fester, the tissue swelling hot and raw around each thread, and the opening would reappear as if her body itself were refusing the verdict he kept trying to impose on it. He did not stop. He sharpened his instruments instead. He adjusted the angle of the speculum he had built from a bent spoon into a proper tool, the very instrument that today still carries his name into operating rooms whose surgeons have likely never heard hers. Each time, Anarcha was laid on her side, knees drawn to her chest, and each time, she endured it without anesthesia — not because ether did not exist. It reached American surgeries in 1846, and Sims used it, later, on the white women who came to him in New York. He did not offer it here. He wrote, in the reasoning of his era, that operations of this kind caused only a moderate distress, that women of her race bore surgical pain with an ease his white patients did not share. It was a conviction, not a discovery. No one in that room was positioned to contradict him.

She was not alone in that ward. Betsey, another young enslaved woman, lay in the bed beside her, recovering from her own repeated operations, her own scars accumulating in silence. Lucy, healed enough now to stand, moved between them changing dressings, holding shoulders down when the pain became a physical thing that fought the body trying to still it. These three women became, in that small building, both patients and staff — nursing each other through agonies inflicted by the same hands that promised, each time, that this attempt would be the one that finally held.

Sims later wrote of this period with something close to tenderness for his own perseverance, describing Anarcha's patience, her fortitude, her willingness to return to the table. He rarely paused to ask what alternative she had ever been given. She could not leave. Her body belonged, by the law of Alabama, to a man who had already loaned her out for this very purpose. Consent, in any form the law would later recognize, was never a question anyone in that room was required to answer. What remained was only repetition — the position, the speculum, the silk, the fever, the slow healing, and then, weeks later, the same walk back to the same table, because Sims was not yet finished, and neither, by his own accounting, was she.

--- PART 6 ---

By the winter of 1849, Sims had performed some thirty operations on Anarcha alone, and every one of them had ended the same way: a wound that closed, and then, within days, quietly opened again. The silk sutures were the failure point, and by then he knew it. Silk held stitches shut on skin, but inside the moist, living tissue of the vaginal wall, it behaved as an irritant, a foreign body the flesh fought to expel, carrying infection with it every time. He had exhausted variation after variation of technique, position, and instrument, and the one variable he had not yet changed was the material holding the wound together at all.

The answer, when it came, arrived almost by accident of substitution. Sims obtained a fine, pliable silver wire — the kind used at the time in jewelry work — and reasoned that a metal inert to the body's tissue might succeed where organic thread had failed for years. In June of 1849, he brought Anarcha to the table one final time, closed the fistula with the silver sutures instead of silk, and waited through the days that had, thirty times before, brought fever and reopening. This time, the wound held. The tissue did not reject the metal. The opening did not return. After four years and thirty operations without anesthesia, Anarcha Wescott's fistula was closed.

Betsey's healed soon after, by the same silver thread. So did Lucy's. Sims had his cure — a technique that would travel from that small Alabama outbuilding into medical journals, into surgical amphitheaters in New York and London and Paris, credited entirely to the surgeon who held the needle and never once to the women who had held still beneath it. He called the discovery his own triumph of perseverance, and in the strictest technical sense, it was true: he had, after years of failure, solved a problem that had defeated physicians for centuries. What he did not write, what no case report of the era required him to write, was the ledger of what that perseverance had cost — the fevers, the infections, the repeated woundings of the same three bodies, endured by women who had no power to refuse the next attempt, no name recorded beside the technique that made him famous, and no silver wire of their own laid down to close what had been opened in them, over and over, in the name of a cure that history would remember as his alone. The instrument was ready now. The surgeon believed himself vindicated. And the world beyond that ward was about to come looking for him.

--- PART 7 ---

The world did come looking, and when it arrived, it arrived hungry. In 1852, Sims sent his findings to the American Journal of the Medical Sciences, and the paper detonated through the profession like a flare over dark water. "On the Treatment of Vesico-Vaginal Fistula" — four years of failure distilled into triumph, silver sutures gleaming on the page as the answer surgeons across two continents had chased for generations. Physicians who had turned patients away, who had told women their condition was simply their cross to bear, now had a name to write on a referral, and that name was J. Marion Sims. He signed the paper alone. Anarcha appeared in it only as a case, a body, a number in a sequence of operations — her endurance reduced to a footnote in the record of his genius. Betsey and Lucy fared no better. The credit, entire and unshared, belonged to the surgeon.

And Sims, feeling the pull of a stage far larger than a clapboard outbuilding in Alabama, followed the momentum north. In 1853, he arrived in New York City with his reputation preceding him, and he set his sights immediately on something no city in America yet possessed — a hospital built entirely for the diseases of women. By 1855, he had it. The Woman's Hospital opened its doors on Madison Avenue, the first institution of its kind in the nation, and Sims stood at its head as founding surgeon, the undisputed architect of a new medical frontier. Wealthy donors poured money into the project. Newspapers praised his compassion. Here, finally, was a man curing the incurable, restoring women who had been abandoned by every other physician in the city.

But behind those hospital walls, the pattern from Alabama did not disappear — it simply changed its uniform. The women who filled the charity wards of the Woman's Hospital were no longer enslaved, but they were poor, largely Irish immigrants, women with no money, no standing, no voice loud enough to reach the men who governed the institution. And Sims operated on them the same way he had operated in Montgomery — without anesthesia, before rotating audiences of visiting physicians who came to witness the famous technique performed live, the fistula repair transformed into demonstration, into spectacle, into proof of American surgical supremacy displayed on the body of a woman too poor to refuse the table. Ether sat in the cabinet. Chloroform sat beside it. And still, again and again, he reached for the same conviction that had carried him through Alabama — that some pain simply mattered less than the cure being built on top of it. The crowd of doctors watching him work grew larger every month. His fame was no longer regional. It was about to become something else entirely.

--- PART 8 ---

By the close of the 1850s, J. Marion Sims was no longer simply a surgeon — he was a phenomenon, and the whole medical world wanted to watch him work. Delegations of physicians crossed oceans to observe his technique firsthand. His silver-suture method spread from New York to London, from Paris to Vienna, cited in journals, taught in lecture halls, replicated in operating theaters where surgeons who had never set foot in Alabama now performed a procedure built on the backs of women whose names they would never learn. And Sims, sensing the scale of the moment, did not stay still. He sailed for Europe, and Europe received him like royalty greeting royalty.

In Paris, in the years before and during the American Civil War, Sims built a private practice among the aristocracy of the continent — treating the wives of dukes, the daughters of ministers, women whose comfort and dignity he now guarded with an entirely different vocabulary. For these patients, anesthesia was standard. For these patients, pain was something to be eliminated, not endured for the sake of demonstrating fortitude. The same hands that had once insisted certain women felt only "moderate" distress now moved with exquisite gentleness across the bodies of Europe's elite, and no one in those glittering salons thought to ask what accounted for the difference. Empress Eugénie of France herself became his patient. His name entered the vocabulary of continental medicine as a marvel imported from the New World.

He returned to America crowned. In 1876, his peers elected him president of the American Medical Association, the highest honor his profession could bestow, a title that sealed, in the eyes of history, his transformation from a struggling frontier doctor into the man the textbooks would soon call the father of modern gynecology. Instruments bearing his name — the Sims speculum, the Sims position, the Sims sigmoid catheter — filled surgical kits across the country, tools so embedded in practice that generations of physicians would use them without a single thought toward Lapsley Street, without a single thought toward Anarcha's thirtieth operation.

And soon, the honors would take physical form. A statue was coming — bronze, monumental, destined to rise in the heart of New York, and later in the capital of the very state where the operations had happened. He would stand in stone, gazing forward, a monument to perseverance and triumph. But stone remembers only what is carved into it, and somewhere beneath every pedestal built in his honor, the ledger he never published was still waiting, patient as Anarcha herself had once been forced to be, for someone, someday, to finally read it aloud.

--- PART 9 ---

J. Marion Sims died in 1883, in New York City, mourned by a profession that owed him almost everything and understood almost none of what that debt had cost. The obituaries called him a genius, a pioneer, a man who had eased the suffering of women for three decades. Not one of them asked whose suffering had eased his own ascent. Eleven years after his death, in 1894, the medical societies of New York raised him in bronze, a statue first placed in Bryant Park, later moved to Central Park, standing at the edge of Fifth Avenue with his gaze fixed forward, dignified, untouchable, exactly the pose of a man certain history had already forgiven him. Another statue rose on the grounds of the South Carolina State House, his birth state claiming him as favorite son. A third stood in Montgomery, Alabama, near the very ground where Anarcha, Betsey, and Lucy had once been carried, screaming, into a wooden outbuilding behind his home. Bronze does not remember pain. Bronze only remembers what a city decides is worth casting in permanence, and for over a century, no one in power decided that Anarcha's name deserved the same treatment.

She had no statue. Neither did Betsey. Neither did Lucy, whose infection from a metal sponge nearly killed her while Sims recorded her recovery as a footnote of professional interest rather than a woman's near-death. Their names survived only in Sims's own writings, buried in his memoir and his surgical papers, mentioned the way a craftsman might mention his tools — present, necessary, unindividuated. For generations, medical schools taught the Sims speculum, the Sims position, without a single lecture on the women whose bodies had been operated upon without anesthesia, sometimes a dozen times, sometimes thirty, in the belief that Black women simply did not feel pain the way white women did — a belief with no basis in physiology, only in the economics of slavery and the conveniences it demanded of medicine.

It took more than a hundred years for that silence to crack. In the late twentieth and early twenty-first centuries, historians, ethicists, and descendants of enslaved communities began pulling the ledger back into daylight — asking, insistently, why the father of gynecology had a monument and his patients had only a case number. Activists gathered outside the Central Park statue with protest signs painted the color of surgical steel. And in 2018, after years of public reckoning, New York City removed the bronze figure from its pedestal altogether, relocating it to a cemetery in Brooklyn where Sims himself is buried — no longer presiding over a park, but returned, finally, to the ground with the dead. The pedestal in Central Park stood empty. And into that emptiness, something else was about to be built.

--- PART 10 ---

In Montgomery, Alabama, not far from where the operations once took place, a different kind of monument finally rose. In 2021, the sculptor and activist Michelle Browder unveiled a towering, three-figure statue titled Mothers of Gynecology — Anarcha, Betsey, and Lucy, rendered not as footnotes but as women, restored to full height, welded from scrap metal gathered from the very community that surrounded them. Anarcha's figure rises tallest of the three, arms open, her face lifted rather than turned away, because Browder built her not as a body upon which history acted, but as a woman who endured, and endured, and endured, until the ground beneath her finally bore witness. For the first time in over a hundred and seventy years, someone in bronze finally spoke her name back into the record, out loud, where it could not be quietly filed away again.

It came too late for Anarcha to hear it. It came too late for Betsey, too late for Lucy, too late for the dozens of other enslaved women whose names never survived at all, who passed beneath J. Marion Sims's hands and vanished from the record entirely, granted not even a first name, not even a case number, only the anonymous plural of "the patients" scattered through his correspondence like debris. Their pain built the foundation of modern gynecology. Their silence built the myth of the man who saved it. And for most of two centuries, medicine chose to remember only the second half of that sentence, and to grieve only for itself.

What remains, now, is the discomfort — the necessary, unresolved discomfort of holding both truths at once. That Sims's technique did, eventually, heal thousands of women from an affliction that had condemned them to isolation and shame. And that the price of that technique was paid, over and over, on tables where consent could not exist, by women whose bodies were never truly their own to offer, whose screams were catalogued as data rather than heard as refusal. There is no clean resolution here. There is only the record, incomplete as it remains, and the quiet obligation to read it more honestly than the century before us ever did.

Because the story of medicine's ambition did not end in that Alabama yard. It simply moved north, and changed its instruments, and found new bodies willing, or unwilling, to bear its cost. As golden statues of the 'father of gynecology' rose in stone across the nation, decades later and further north, another physician would take up the scalpel — not for the womb, but for the mouth, the gut, and the spine, convinced that madness itself was a rotting infection to be cut out at any cost.
